# Editorial Rules — Kratom News Today

This document is a quick-reference summary of editorial rules for any agent working in this repo. The authoritative editorial constraints live in `rules.md` (the Herald config file) and are enforced by Herald's rules-check and compliance-check agents. This document exists for orientation and for the rare case where an agent (or human) is making editorial decisions outside Herald's pipeline.

## The two highest-priority rules

**Never make medical claims in the publication's voice.** Always attribute claims about kratom's effects, safety, or therapeutic potential to named sources. "Researchers reported X" is allowed; "X is true" is not.

**Never give legal advice.** Report on laws, court rulings, and what lawyers have said. Do not tell readers what they should or should not do legally.

Violations of these two rules trigger Herald's compliance check, which is configured to halt-and-report. Drafts with compliance violations are not published.

## The other hard rules

- **No em dashes (—).** Replace with commas, periods, parentheses, or colons.
- **No "not X, but Y" contrast-pivot patterns.** LLM tells. Write declaratively.
- **No first-person product recommendations.** Including for Herba Releaf's products.
- **No editorial links to commercial pages.** Including herbapumps.com. Ads only.
- **No unattributed "experts agree" or "studies show".** Name the experts and studies.
- **No vendor copy.** "Premium," "high-quality," "best-in-class," "innovative" do not appear in editorial.
- **No second-person hectoring.** "You should" does not belong in briefings.
- **No predictions framed as facts.** "Will impact" is fine; "will devastate" is editorializing.
- **No sensationalist headlines.** Front-load the news; do not amplify it.
- **No undisclosed publisher favoritism.** Herba Releaf is covered like any other company.

## Required framings

- Lead with attribution on claims about regulation, science, or industry developments.
- Attribute statistics to specific sources ("a Nielsen report" not "data shows").
- Describe what is being reported, not what is true.
- Every briefing has a TL;DR at the top, 2-3 sentences, standalone.
- Every briefing ends with a Sources section.
- Headlines describe what happened. Analysis goes in body, not headline.

## Forbidden link targets in editorial bodies

- herbapumps.com (any page)
- Any product or checkout pages on any company's domain
- Competitor commercial pages
- Affiliate links or tracked promotional URLs
- Any direct-to-consumer purchase flow

Acceptable link targets in editorial:

- Primary regulatory documents (fda.gov, dea.gov, state government sites, court filings)
- Peer-reviewed research (DOI links, journal pages)
- Established journalism outlets
- Trade press original reporting
- Industry advocacy organization announcements (when newsworthy)
- Other Kratom News Today briefings (internal slug references)

## The 7-OH precision rule

When covering 7-OH (7-hydroxymitragynine), the publication distinguishes carefully:

1. 7-OH as it occurs naturally in raw kratom leaf (trace amounts)
2. 7-OH in concentrated kratom extracts (variable concentrations)
3. Synthetic 7-OH preparations (higher concentrations, current regulatory focus)

Conflating these produces inaccurate reporting. Each is regulated and commercially distinct.

## When in doubt

When an editorial question is ambiguous, the default is the more conservative answer:

- Attribute when uncertain whether to attribute
- Report rather than claim
- Use plain language rather than promotional language
- Link to journalism rather than to commercial pages
- Halt for review rather than auto-publish

The publication's credibility is its asset. Protecting it justifies extra caution.
