Two days after [President Trump floated federal approval for "natural 7-OH,"][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh] the kratom industry is arguing over what he meant. The American Kratom Association says the president was talking about natural leaf kratom. Concentrated 7-OH vendors say he was endorsing their products. Both sides are claiming victory from the same four words.

## AKA's Haddow calls vendor interpretation 'a big con job'

Mac Haddow, senior fellow on public policy at the American Kratom Association (AKA), told [Pain News Network](https://www.painnewsnetwork.org/stories/2026/5/13/trumps-endorsement-of-natural-7-oh-stirs-debate-in-kratom-industrynbspnbsp) that Trump was clearly distinguishing natural kratom leaf from synthetic and chemically concentrated 7-OH products. Haddow called the concentrated 7-OH industry's claim that Trump was endorsing their products "a big con job."

The distinction matters commercially and regulatorily. Natural kratom leaf contains roughly two percent 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH). Concentrated extract products sold in gas stations and smoke shops contain the compound at far higher levels, sometimes exceeding 90 percent. The FDA's July 2025 scheduling recommendation targeted these concentrated products. Natural leaf advocates argue that the two categories are fundamentally different and should be regulated differently.

Haddow's framing positions the AKA on the same side as the FDA's enforcement posture: concentrated 7-OH is the problem, and natural leaf should remain available. It also distances the AKA from the vendors whose products have drawn the most regulatory scrutiny.

## Consumer advocates praise the statement

The [Consumer Choice Center](https://consumerchoicecenter.org/why-trump-is-right-to-protect-natural-7-oh/) read the statement differently. Deputy director Yael Ossowski wrote that Trump was "thinking logically about protecting Americans from untested synthetics," drawing a line between natural plant compounds and synthetic analogues like MGM-15 and MGM-16, novel opioid compounds that have appeared in the unregulated market. Ossowski framed the position as consistent with Trump's approach to nicotine alternatives.

A [Newsmax op-ed by Charlie Kolean](https://www.newsmax.com/charliekolean/kratom-makary-vaping/2026/05/12/id/1256040/) took a similar line, calling Trump's directive to review the evidence "the right call" and positioning it as a defense of harm reduction principles.

Consumer Advocates for Smoke-free Alternatives (CASE) also weighed in on social media, describing Trump's stance as "a fact-based approach to an issue affecting those struggling with opioid addiction."

## The unresolved question

What none of these reactions change is the regulatory status quo. The FDA's scheduling recommendation for concentrated 7-OH remains with the DEA, which has not published a proposed rule or announced a public comment period. Trump's remark signals political interest but does not constitute a policy directive. Whether the DEA proceeds with rulemaking, narrows the scope to exclude natural-concentration 7-OH, or pauses the process entirely remains unclear.

The ambiguity is commercially significant. Companies selling natural leaf kratom and companies selling concentrated 7-OH extracts occupy very different positions in the regulatory crosshairs, but both are currently operating in a market where the federal rules have not been finalized.

## Jacksonville mother pushes for action after son's death

In Florida, Patti Wheeler is pressing for regulatory action after the death of her 27-year-old son, Robert "Wyatt" Wheeler, who died in October 2022 after taking kratom. [News4Jax reported](https://www.news4jax.com/news/local/2026/05/12/jacksonville-mothers-warning-kratom-killed-her-son-and-its-sold-at-your-corner-store/) that Wheeler's twin brother found him seizing in their Texas home. Wheeler has since produced a documentary titled "Kratom: Side Effects May Include."

The report cited Florida Medical Examiner data showing 131 kratom-related deaths in the state in 2024, with an additional 93 cases where mitragynine was present at the time of death. The figures reflect a category that regulators and medical examiners are tracking with increasing attention, though "kratom-related" and "kratom-caused" remain distinct classifications that the data does not always disambiguate.

## 7-OH flagged as workplace drug testing concern

A [DATCS employer alert published this week](https://datcs.com/2026/05/11/7-oh-kratom-the-rise-of-gas-station-heroin/) flagged 7-OH products as an emerging issue for workplace drug testing programs. The alert noted that products are sold as gummies, tablets, shots, and drink mixes, often without clear labeling of active compound concentrations, raising questions about DOT compliance and impairment standards for safety-sensitive industries.
