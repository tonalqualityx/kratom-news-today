Ohio became the latest state to formally classify synthetic kratom as a controlled substance, and New Hampshire's House passed a bill targeting synthetic and high-concentration kratom products. Both actions landed on the same day, extending a wave of state-level regulation that has accelerated since [Utah's Kratom Regulation Act took effect earlier this month][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate].

## Ohio's permanent synthetic kratom ban replaces emergency order

Ohio's permanent ban on synthetic kratom products took effect on May 14, according to [The Statehouse News Bureau](https://www.statenews.org/government-politics/2026-05-19/ban-on-synthetic-kratom-in-effect-in-ohio-but-more-action-could-be-coming). The rule, approved by the Joint Committee on Agency Rule Review (JCARR) following an April hearing, classifies synthetic kratom as a Schedule 1 controlled substance in the state. It replaces a temporary emergency ban issued by the Ohio Board of Pharmacy that was set to expire in June.

The Governor's Executive Order specifically exempts natural kratom in vegetation form, drawing the same line between natural leaf and synthetic concentrates that several other states have adopted in recent months.

The Board of Pharmacy is still considering whether to extend the ban to natural kratom, [ABC6 reported](https://abc6onyourside.com/news/local/growing-crackdown-more-states-ban-synthetic-kratom-or-7-oh-ohio-before-federal-government). That question remains open and could produce a second round of regulatory action in Ohio later this year.

At a March hearing before the House Agriculture Committee, Tom Beckham with the 7 Hope Alliance, a pro-kratom advocacy group, argued that banning something people in pain rely on will not end demand. Beckham told legislators the ban would instead create a black market, according to The Statehouse News Bureau.

## New Hampshire House passes SB557

The New Hampshire House passed SB557 on May 14 by a vote of 176 to 157, [NHPR reported](https://www.nhpr.org/nh-news/2026-05-06/kratom-nh-lawmakers-look-to-regulate-kratom-potency). As amended, the bill would make it illegal to prepare, distribute, manufacture, sell, possess, or advertise synthetic and semisynthetic kratom, with exceptions for scientific research.

The bill also targets concentrated natural products. Under SB557, any kratom product containing more than 1,000 parts per million (0.1%) of 7-hydroxymitragynine by dry weight would be illegal to sell in New Hampshire, according to [the bill text on LegiScan](https://legiscan.com/NH/text/SB557/id/3394786). That threshold sits well above the trace concentrations found in natural kratom leaf, which typically contains roughly two percent mitragynine and far smaller amounts of 7-OH, but would cover most concentrated extract products currently on the market.

The state Senate had approved a different version of the bill. The two chambers will need to resolve their differences before sending a final version to Gov. Kelly Ayotte, who has expressed support for kratom regulation, the [Monadnock Ledger-Transcript reported](https://ledgertranscript.com/2026/05/12/high-dose-kratom-ban-bill-supported/).

## The regulatory pattern

Ohio and New Hampshire join a growing list of states drawing regulatory lines around synthetic and concentrated kratom products while leaving natural leaf available. [Utah's framework][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate], which took effect May 6, takes a different approach by regulating all kratom through registration and purity standards rather than banning specific product categories. Meanwhile, [cities in Washington state][washington-local-governments-fill-kratom-regulatory-vacuum] have been passing local ordinances in the absence of state-level action.

The common thread across these approaches is the distinction between natural kratom leaf and concentrated or synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine preparations, a distinction that [President Trump's recent remarks on "natural 7-OH"][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh] brought to the federal conversation as well. How the federal process resolves that distinction will shape whether the current state-by-state patchwork holds or becomes preempted.
