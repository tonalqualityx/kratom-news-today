**TL;DR:** New York Senator Scarcella-Spanton introduced the "Synthetic Kratom Kills Act" (S10514), which would ban synthetic kratom products while explicitly preserving access to natural leaf. The same week, a documentary about kratom-related deaths premieres in Jacksonville, and Florida death data continues to draw scrutiny from investigators and lawmakers.

## New York targets synthetic kratom with S10514

Senator Scarcella-Spanton introduced [S10514, the "Synthetic Kratom Kills Act,"](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/S10514) on May 15. The bill would prohibit the manufacture, distribution, and sale of synthesized kratom material and adulterated kratom products in New York. It would also ban kratom products intended for combustion or vaporization, kratom sold in conventional food and beverages (with an exception for traditional tea), kratom with flavoring designed to appeal to consumers under 21, and any kratom product not packaged in child-resistant packaging. Sales to anyone under 21 would be prohibited.

The bill explicitly preserves access to natural kratom leaf and acknowledges its use by veterans managing pain and PTSD. The sponsor described the approach as a "smarter" alternative to a blanket ban: prohibit synthetic products, regulate natural ones.

New York is not starting from scratch. In December 2025, Governor Hochul signed legislation requiring warning labels on kratom products and prohibiting sales to minors. S10514 goes further by drawing a hard line against synthetic formulations, following the same natural-versus-synthetic distinction that [Ohio codified when its permanent ban took effect on May 14][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect] and that [Utah addressed through its registration framework][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate].

## Documentary on kratom deaths premieres in Jacksonville

"Kratom: Side Effects May Include," a documentary about kratom-related fatalities, premiered at the historic Ritz Theatre in Jacksonville on May 16. The film was directed by Jamie Neese and Jason Neese and is set to screen at the [Dances With Films festival](https://danceswithfilms.com/kratom-side-effects-may-include/).

The documentary's executive producer is Patti Wheeler, whose 27-year-old son Robert "Wyatt" Wheeler died on October 25, 2022, after taking kratom. [News4Jax reported](https://www.news4jax.com/news/local/2026/05/12/jacksonville-mothers-warning-kratom-killed-her-son-and-its-sold-at-your-corner-store/) that Wheeler's twin brother Gannon found him having a seizure. The [Deseret News covered](https://www.deseret.com/utah/2026/02/20/kratom-harm-documentary-screening/) the film's earlier screening and Wheeler's advocacy work.

[Mountainside Treatment Center](https://www.mountainside.com/news/releases/kratom-film-cultural-reckoning/) called the documentary a "defining cultural moment" for public awareness of kratom's risks. Whether the film shifts the broader conversation remains to be seen, but its premiere coincides with an active period of state-level regulatory action.

## Florida death data adds context

The documentary's Jacksonville premiere lands against a backdrop of Florida-specific mortality data that has drawn increasing attention. According to [News4Jax](https://www.news4jax.com/news/local/2026/05/12/jacksonville-mothers-warning-kratom-killed-her-son-and-its-sold-at-your-corner-store/), the Florida Medical Examiner's Commission reported that 131 people died in the state in 2024 with mitragynine listed as a cause of death. An additional 93 decedents had the compound present in their system at the time of death.

A [Tampa Bay Times investigation](https://project.tampabay.com/investigations/deadly-dose/kratom-overdose-deaths-florida-mitragynine-testing/) found more than 580 kratom-related deaths in Florida over the past decade. Of those, 46 cases involved kratom as the only substance detected. The distinction between "kratom-related" (present at death) and "kratom-only" (sole substance) is significant. Polydrug cases complicate causal attribution, and medical examiners have noted the difficulty of isolating kratom's role when other substances are involved.

Florida does not currently have statewide kratom-specific legislation comparable to the bills moving in New York, Ohio, or [Utah][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate]. The death data and the documentary's Florida premiere may increase pressure on state lawmakers, though no bill has been introduced as of this writing.

## The emerging regulatory pattern

New York's S10514 reinforces a pattern now visible across multiple states: target synthetic and concentrated kratom products while leaving natural leaf accessible under some form of regulation. [Ohio][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect] took the scheduling route. [Utah][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate] built a registration and purity framework. New York's bill would layer synthetic prohibitions on top of existing labeling and age-restriction requirements. Each approach reflects the same underlying judgment that natural leaf and synthetic or concentrated products present different risk profiles and warrant different regulatory treatment, a distinction that [President Trump's recent remarks][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh] brought into the federal conversation.
