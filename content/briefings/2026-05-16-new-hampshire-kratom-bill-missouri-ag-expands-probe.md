**TL;DR:** New Hampshire's House passed a bill Thursday banning the sale of kratom products with enhanced 7-hydroxymitragynine levels through Liquor Commission licensees. Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway widened her kratom investigation to 22 firms, targeting the full supply chain, though a judge earlier this month denied her bid to halt sales by CBD American Shaman.

## New Hampshire House targets enhanced 7-OH products

The New Hampshire House passed legislation on Thursday that would prohibit any licensee of the state's Liquor Commission from selling kratom products containing "enhanced levels" of 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) beyond what occurs naturally in the kratom leaf. The [Concord Monitor reported](https://www.concordmonitor.com/2026/05/16/kratom-regulation-new-hampshire/) on the bill as part of a broader feature examining New Hampshire's evolving relationship with kratom regulation.

The bill arrives years after Franklin, NH became one of the earliest municipalities in the country to ban kratom sales within city limits in 2019. State lawmakers are now attempting to address the category at the state level, joining a growing list of legislatures drawing distinctions between natural kratom leaf and products with concentrated or synthetic 7-OH. [Ohio's ban on synthetic kratom products took effect May 14][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect], and [Utah's Kratom Regulation Act][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate] set a similar regulatory framework earlier this year.

Mark Watman, vice president and chief operating officer of the recovery center Archways, told the Concord Monitor that he has noticed more people experiencing issues with kratom over the past two years. Addiction counselors and other experts cited in the piece described kratom use as rising, though the Monitor's reporting focused on enhanced 7-OH products rather than natural leaf.

The distinction between natural and enhanced 7-OH is the regulatory fault line in most current state legislation. Natural kratom leaf contains roughly two percent 7-OH. Enhanced products sold in retail settings contain the compound at concentrations well above those levels. The New Hampshire bill targets the latter category specifically.

## Missouri AG expands kratom probe to 22 entities

Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway expanded her statewide investigation into kratom and 7-OH products, adding ten entities and bringing the total under investigation to 22, according to the [Missouri AG's office](https://ago.mo.gov/attorney-general-hanaway-expands-kratom-investigation-to-target-supply-chain/). The expansion targets manufacturers, distributors, and retailers across the supply chain.

The expanded probe uses Civil Investigative Demands (CIDs), which require the targeted businesses to produce documentation covering operations, product labeling, marketing practices, and ingredient information. [KTTN reported](https://www.kttn.com/attorney-general-widens-missouri-kratom-investigation-to-22-firms/) that the investigation now spans the full commercial pipeline from production to point of sale.

State health data cited by the AG's office shows synthetic 7-OH was involved in at least 197 deaths in Missouri. The figures underscore the enforcement rationale, though the data does not specify whether the deaths involved synthetic 7-OH alone or in combination with other substances.

The expansion follows Hanaway's March 31 lawsuit against CBD American Shaman. A Jackson County judge on May 8 [denied the AG's request](https://www.kcur.org/health/2026-05-08/judge-denies-missouri-attorney-generals-bid-to-halt-7-oh-kratom-sales-by-american-shaman) for a temporary restraining order that would have immediately halted the company's 7-OH product sales. KCUR reported that the judge found "competing affidavits" from experts on both sides, an evidentiary split that prevented the court from granting emergency relief. The denial did not end the underlying lawsuit, which continues.

## A widening state-level pattern

New Hampshire and Missouri represent different regulatory approaches (legislation versus enforcement action) aimed at the same category of products. Both focus on enhanced or synthetic 7-OH rather than natural kratom leaf. Combined with [Ohio's synthetic kratom ban][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect], [Utah's registration framework][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate], and [local bans in Washington state][washington-local-governments-fill-kratom-regulatory-vacuum], the state-level regulatory picture continues to develop along a consistent axis: concentrated 7-OH products face increasing scrutiny while natural leaf kratom draws comparatively less legislative attention.
