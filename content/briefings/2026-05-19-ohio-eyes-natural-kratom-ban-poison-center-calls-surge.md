## Ohio synthetic ban is permanent; natural kratom may be next

The [Statehouse News Bureau reported](https://www.statenews.org/government-politics/2026-05-19/ban-on-synthetic-kratom-in-effect-in-ohio-but-more-action-could-be-coming) on Monday that Ohio's permanent ban on synthetic kratom is now in effect, replacing the temporary emergency ban the Board of Pharmacy issued [earlier this month][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect]. The permanent rule codifies what the emergency order established: synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) preparations are prohibited from sale in the state.

The larger development in the piece is what comes next. The Ohio Board of Pharmacy is considering whether to extend restrictions to natural kratom as well, according to the Statehouse News Bureau. Multiple medical experts and state lawmakers quoted in the report described all forms of kratom as dangerous. If the Board moves forward, Ohio would join a small number of states that restrict natural kratom leaf alongside synthetic derivatives.

At a March hearing before the Ohio House Agriculture Committee, Tom Beckham of the 7 Hope Alliance argued that banning something people in pain rely on would drive consumers to an unregulated black market, according to the Statehouse News Bureau's account of the hearing.

## State-level landscape continues to fragment

Ohio's permanent ban adds to a growing and increasingly complex patchwork of state regulation. At least 12 states have enacted full bans on 7-OH products: Alabama, Arkansas, California, Florida, Indiana, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Rhode Island, Tennessee, Vermont, and Wisconsin. Several others, including Arizona, Colorado, South Carolina, Texas, and West Virginia, have implemented restrictions targeting high-potency formulations specifically.

At the federal level, the FDA recommended eight months ago that the DEA classify synthetic 7-OH as a Schedule I substance. The DEA has not yet issued a ruling. As [previously reported][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh], President Trump's recent remarks about "natural 7-OH" have added a political dimension to the federal timeline, though the remarks do not constitute a policy directive.

## CDC data: poison center calls up 1,200% over a decade

A CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) published March 26 continues to generate press coverage. The [report](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7511a1.htm), led by researchers at the University of Virginia and Baylor College of Medicine, found that kratom-related calls to U.S. poison centers increased from 258 in 2015 to 3,434 in 2025, an increase of roughly 1,200%.

Hospitalizations tracked a similar trajectory, rising from 43 cases in 2015 to 538 in 2025. The researchers identified 233 deaths associated with kratom use over the study period. Of those deaths, 184 (79%) involved multiple substances, a detail that complicates any single-cause attribution.

Males accounted for 65 to 76 percent of reports across the study period. Adults aged 20 to 39 represented the most reported age group, though the 40-to-59 age group climbed steeply over the decade.

A second MMWR report, published April 2, found that kava-related poison center calls rose 383% during a similar period. Roughly 30 percent of those kava-related calls also involved kratom, [StudyFinds reported](https://studyfinds.com/kratom-poison-center-reports-up-tremendously/).

## What to watch

Ohio's Board of Pharmacy has not announced a timeline for any decision on natural kratom. If the Board does move to restrict natural leaf, Ohio would become a test case for how far state pharmacy boards can extend scheduling authority into a category where the federal government has not yet acted. The DEA's pending review of the FDA's synthetic 7-OH scheduling recommendation remains the dominant federal storyline, and states like Ohio are filling the regulatory gap in the meantime.
