A cabinet secretary in the Trump administration holds a significant financial stake in a kratom company while the administration pursues federal scheduling of a key kratom alkaloid. Meanwhile, two outlets published pieces amplifying health officials' alarm over concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products sold at convenience stores and gas stations across the country.

## DHS Secretary Mullin holds up to $1M in Botanic Tonics

Department of Homeland Security Secretary Markwayne Mullin owns between $500,000 and $1 million in shares of Botanic Tonics LLC, the Oklahoma-based company behind Feel Free, a kratom-and-kava tonic sold in more than 23,000 retail outlets, [The Daily Beast reported](https://www.thedailybeast.com/ice-cowboy-dhs-secretary-markwayne-mullin-holds-1m-in-kratom-gas-station-heroin-maker/). The holding was disclosed in Mullin's U.S. Office of Government Ethics filing.

The disclosure raises conflict-of-interest questions. Mullin spent last summer campaigning alongside HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. for a federal crackdown on kratom. Kennedy and FDA Commissioner Marty Makary recommended classifying synthetic 7-OH as a Schedule I substance in July 2025, a move that would reshape the competitive landscape for companies like Botanic Tonics.

Botanic Tonics has its own complicated regulatory history. The FDA seized roughly $3 million in Botanic Tonics products (including Feel Free capsules and bulk kratom) in 2023, according to The Daily Beast. That forfeiture case was still in litigation as of 2025. The company is also one of several firms that [sued to block Utah's Kratom Regulation Act][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate], though a federal judge denied the injunction.

The overlap between Mullin's financial interest and the administration's policy posture on kratom sits at the center of the story. A cabinet secretary holding a six- to seven-figure position in a kratom company, while the administration he serves in pursues scheduling policy that would directly affect the competitive dynamics of that company's market, is the kind of entanglement that draws scrutiny from ethics watchdogs and congressional oversight alike.

## ConsumerAffairs reports on "gas station heroin" concerns

[ConsumerAffairs published a piece](https://www.consumeraffairs.com/news/gas-station-heroin-raises-alarm-among-health-officials-051126.html) detailing health officials' warnings about concentrated 7-OH products sold in convenience stores nationwide. The article used the phrase "gas station heroin," a term that has gained traction among regulators and in media coverage of the category.

Health officials quoted in the piece warned that concentrated 7-OH products can cause addiction, respiratory depression, seizures, and fatal overdoses, according to ConsumerAffairs. Addiction specialists cited in the article said consumers often underestimate the risks because the products sit on shelves alongside energy drinks and dietary supplements.

The distinction between these concentrated products and traditional kratom leaf matters. Natural kratom contains 7-OH in trace amounts. The products drawing regulatory and media attention contain the alkaloid at far higher concentrations, whether through extraction or synthesis, a difference the [ongoing federal policy conversation][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh] and [state-level bans][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect] have attempted to address with varying precision.

## Arizona Capitol Times: state laws lag behind the market

An op-ed in the [Arizona Capitol Times](https://azcapitoltimes.com/news/2026/05/11/gas-station-heroin-is-putting-arizona-families-at-risk-our-laws-havent-kept-up/) argued that Arizona's state laws have not kept pace with the proliferation of concentrated 7-OH products. The piece cited Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes, who previously warned about dangerous products containing concentrated 7-OH.

The article highlighted the case of Phoenix-area parents who pushed for a kratom ban after a 17-year-old's rehab stay, according to the Arizona Capitol Times. Arizona has not yet passed legislation specifically targeting kratom or 7-OH products, placing it among the states where the category remains largely unregulated at the state level.

## The broader picture

The Mullin disclosure arrives at a moment when federal kratom policy is actively being shaped. The [Trump administration has signaled interest][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh] in distinguishing between natural and synthetic 7-OH, and the [industry itself is divided][industry-splits-over-trump-natural-7-oh-remarks] over how that distinction should be drawn. A cabinet secretary's financial stake in one of the companies most directly affected by these decisions adds another dimension to an already complicated policy landscape.
