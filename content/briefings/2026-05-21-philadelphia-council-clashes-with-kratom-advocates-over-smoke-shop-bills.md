**TL;DR:** Philadelphia City Council held a heated, three-plus-hour hearing on two bills that would classify kratom and hemp-derived THC as "intoxicating substances," require special licensing for sellers, and restrict buyers to age 21 and older. Kratom advocates warned the bills would function as a de facto ban, while community leaders backed the measures as a tool to curb smoke-shop-related crime. The city's broad approach contrasts sharply with pending state legislation in Pennsylvania and New Jersey, which targets synthetic and concentrated 7-OH products while leaving natural kratom leaf alone.

## Council hearing draws nearly two dozen speakers

Philadelphia City Council's May 21 hearing on two bills introduced by Majority Leader Katherine Gilmore-Richardson ran more than three hours and drew nearly two dozen speakers, [the Inquirer reported](https://www.inquirer.com/news/philadelphia/kratom-philadelphia-city-council-bill-regulation-20260521.html). The more contentious of the two bills would define kratom and hemp-based THC products as "intoxicating substances," requiring sellers to obtain a special license, comply with testing requirements, and verify that buyers are at least 21 years old.

Gilmore-Richardson framed the bills as a response to neighborhood complaints about smoke shops. "These businesses fuel crime," she told the hearing, according to [FOX 29](https://www.fox29.com/news/philly-leaders-target-businesses-selling-kratom-delta-8-products). Community leaders from West Philadelphia, Strawberry Mansion, and Queen Village testified in support, describing smoke shops as magnets for disorder and arguing that tighter regulation would improve public safety, [PhillyVoice reported](https://www.phillyvoice.com/philly-smoke-shops-kratom-hemp-legislation/).

## Kratom industry pushes back hard

Opposition centered on the bill's sweeping definition of "intoxicating substance." Allison Smith of the Global Kratom Coalition told the hearing that the legislation would amount to a "de facto ban of all kratom products in Philadelphia" by requiring sellers of natural leaf to comply with what she described as pharmaceutical-grade testing and licensing regulations, according to the Inquirer.

The kratom industry has been lobbying at both the state and city level to distinguish natural kratom leaf from synthetic and concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products. That distinction is central to [pending state-level legislation][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect] in multiple states, and to the [broader national debate][industry-splits-over-trump-natural-7-oh-remarks] over how to regulate the category. Philadelphia's approach, which treats all kratom as an intoxicating substance regardless of form, sidesteps that distinction entirely.

## Philadelphia's bills diverge from state-level approach

Pennsylvania and New Jersey are both considering legislation that would ban 7-OH products at the state level. Those bills would not outlaw natural leaf kratom. Philadelphia's proposed framework is more aggressive: it would subject all kratom products, including unprocessed leaf, to the same licensing and testing regime applied to hemp-derived THC concentrates.

That gap creates a potential conflict. If Pennsylvania passes a state-level bill that explicitly permits natural kratom while Philadelphia's ordinance treats it as an intoxicating substance, the regulatory landscape for sellers operating across city and state lines could become difficult to navigate. Similar tensions between local and state approaches have already emerged in [Washington state][washington-local-governments-fill-kratom-regulatory-vacuum], where cities passed local ordinances after the legislature failed to act.

## Inquirer investigation adds context on formula evasion

The hearing comes weeks after a May 5 Inquirer investigation revealed that kratom companies are already changing their formulas to sidestep pending state bans on 7-OH. At an Atlantic City smoke shop expo, half a dozen 7-OH sales reps told the [Inquirer](https://www.inquirer.com/news/kratom-ban-7oh-pennsylvania-new-jersey-20260505.html) their companies were reformulating products to avoid the new laws.

One sales rep, Matt Swann, was promoting a new product called "Cori" that wraps its active compound in corydalis, an East Asian plant with pain-killing alkaloids that is not targeted by any proposed kratom ban. Pennsylvania's pending bill mimics Vermont's strategy: it would outlaw products containing more than two percent 7-OH along with any "synthetic, semi-synthetic, or chemically manipulated alkaloid" derived from kratom. Corydalis-derived extracts, however, may fall outside that definition entirely.

That kind of formula evasion is part of what makes Philadelphia's broader approach appealing to regulators. By defining the category as "intoxicating substances" rather than targeting specific alkaloids, the city's bills could cover products that state-level bans might miss. The tradeoff, as kratom advocates argued at the hearing, is that the same breadth sweeps in natural leaf products that other jurisdictions have chosen to leave alone.

## What comes next

The bills remain in committee. Council members gave no timeline for a vote, according to the Inquirer. The outcome will depend in part on whether Pennsylvania's state-level 7-OH ban advances, since a state law could either preempt or complicate Philadelphia's local approach. For the kratom industry, the Philadelphia hearing is the latest front in a [widening regulatory patchwork][new-hampshire-kratom-bill-missouri-ag-expands-probe] that now spans city councils, state legislatures, and the [federal executive branch][dhs-secretary-mullin-holds-stake-in-kratom-company].
