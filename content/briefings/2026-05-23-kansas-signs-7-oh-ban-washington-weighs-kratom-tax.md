Kansas Gov. Laura Kelly signed legislation adding 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) to the state's Schedule I controlled substances list, while a broader bill that would have scheduled all kratom died in committee. Separately, Washington lawmakers are advancing two bills that would impose a 95% tax on kratom and set a minimum purchase age of 21, taking a regulatory approach rather than a ban. Alabama's attorney general issued a statewide cease-and-desist order targeting businesses selling kratom products containing mitragynine or 7-OH.

## Kansas Schedules 7-OH, Declines to Ban Kratom Leaf

Kansas Gov. Laura Kelly signed [House Bill 2365](https://www.governor.ks.gov/Home/Components/News/News/1016/55) into law, adding 7-OH kratom-related substances to Schedule I under the state's Uniform Controlled Substances Act. According to the governor's office, the bill also adds several other substances to the state schedules, including fentanyl-related substances and hexahydrocannabinol.

Kelly said the FDA is still evaluating kratom and that Kansas should "safeguard the health of Kansans" until scientific reviews demonstrate benefits with minimal risk, according to the governor's office press release.

A separate bill, [SB 497](https://kslegislature.gov/b2025_26/bills/SB497/), which would have added kratom itself to Schedule I, is listed as "Died" in the Kansas Legislature's current session records. The distinction matters: Kansas chose to target concentrated 7-OH products while leaving natural kratom leaf legal, a pattern that mirrors the approach taken by [Ohio's permanent synthetic kratom ban][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect] and the FDA's own regulatory posture separating concentrated 7-OH from natural leaf.

The signing follows a week of accelerating state and federal pressure on kratom. On Thursday, a [bipartisan group of senators urged the FDA][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down] to pursue scheduling for all kratom, arguing that targeting only 7-OH would leave addictive products on shelves.

## Washington Advances Tax-and-Age Regulation

Washington state lawmakers are advancing two kratom-related bills through the legislature, according to [local news coverage](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MTCGIC2bbgU). One bill would impose a 95% tax on kratom products. The other would require buyers to be 21 or older.

Neither bill has been enacted, but the approach represents a different regulatory philosophy from Kansas's scheduling action. Where Kansas classified 7-OH as a controlled substance, Washington is considering tax-and-age-restriction regulation that would keep kratom products legal but significantly increase their cost and restrict their availability.

A [Rockefeller Institute analysis](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/) published this month described the state-level landscape as a patchwork: some states classify kratom alkaloids as Schedule I substances, others regulate with age limits and product standards, and many continue to consider new bills. A [Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association summary](https://legislativeanalysis.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Kratom-Summary-of-State-Laws.pdf) reached similar conclusions.

## Alabama AG Issues Statewide Cease-and-Desist

Alabama's attorney general issued a [statewide cease-and-desist order](https://www.alabamaag.gov/alabama-attorney-general-issues-statewide-cease-and-desist-order-targeting-illegal-kratom-products/) targeting businesses possessing or selling products containing mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine. The attorney general's office said both compounds are Schedule I controlled substances under Alabama law.

The order follows enforcement actions in other states. Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway recently [expanded her office's kratom investigation to 22 entities][new-hampshire-kratom-bill-missouri-ag-expands-probe], targeting manufacturers, distributors, and retailers across the supply chain. Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton has also [filed suit against kratom retailers][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down].

The Alabama action is notable because it targets both mitragynine and 7-OH, covering natural kratom products alongside concentrated preparations. Most recent state enforcement actions have focused primarily on 7-OH.
