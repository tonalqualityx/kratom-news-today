## New York enacts age limit and warning labels

[Governor Hochul signed two kratom bills](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-signs-two-pieces-landmark-legislation-protect-new-yorkers-against-harmful) on Friday, according to the governor's office. One law prohibits the sale of kratom products to anyone under 21. The other requires warning labels on all kratom products sold, manufactured, or distributed in the state.

Hochul described the legislation as protecting New Yorkers from the "harmful effects" of kratom, according to the press release. A sponsoring lawmaker called the age restriction a "first logical step" toward broader regulation.

New York's approach follows the consumer-protection model that several states have adopted in recent months, setting purchase-age floors and labeling requirements rather than outright bans. The state had [previously introduced the "Synthetic Kratom Kills Act"][new-york-synthetic-kratom-kills-act-documentary-premieres] earlier this month, a separate bill targeting synthetic kratom specifically. The bills Hochul signed apply to all kratom products regardless of formulation.

The [bill text for A05852](https://assembly.state.ny.us/leg/?Actions=Y&Memo=Y&Summary=Y&Text=Y&Votes=Y&bn=A05852&term=) is available on the New York Assembly's website.

## Michigan House advances outright ban

The Michigan House passed HB 5537 this week, according to [local news coverage](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7K7WF29v2tg). The bill would ban the growth, sale, and import of kratom in Michigan if it clears the Senate and is signed by the governor.

The vote fell mostly along party lines, with Democratic legislators favoring regulation over prohibition, according to the same report. The bill now moves to the Michigan Senate, where its prospects remain unclear.

Michigan's approach contrasts sharply with the regulatory frameworks adopted in states like New York, [Kansas][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax], and Utah. Where those states have targeted specific compounds (particularly 7-hydroxymitragynine) or set age and labeling requirements, Michigan's bill would prohibit the entire category.

## Broader state landscape continues to fragment

The two actions this week underscore the divergence in state approaches that [federal lawmakers flagged earlier this month][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down]. A bipartisan group of senators urged the FDA to provide federal clarity, arguing that the state-by-state patchwork leaves consumers and businesses without consistent standards.

The California Department of Public Health also issued guidance this week stating that foods and dietary supplements containing kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) are illegal to sell or manufacture for consumption in California, according to a CDPH advisory. The guidance aligns with the FDA's position that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a dietary supplement or food additive.

According to the [Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association](https://legislativeanalysis.org/kratom-summary-of-state-laws/), which maintains a state-by-state tracker, at least six states now ban kratom outright, while more than a dozen others have enacted age restrictions, labeling requirements, or targeted 7-OH bans.
