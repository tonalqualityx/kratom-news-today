## TL;DR

The Arizona Senate passed HB 2415, a bill that would raise the state's kratom purchase age to 21, ban synthetically derived kratom products, and classify high-concentration 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products as narcotic drugs. Separately, Delaware is considering SB 262, which would place kratom into the state's controlled-substances framework. Federal marshals also seized more than 100 cases of kratom products in California.

## Arizona Senate Advances Age-21, Synthetic-Ban Framework

The Arizona Senate passed [HB 2415](https://www.azleg.gov/legtext/57leg/2R/summary/H.HB2415_012926_SENATEENGROSSED.DOCX.htm), a bill sponsored by Representative Nguyen that would overhaul the state's approach to kratom regulation, according to the bill's engrossed summary published by the Arizona Legislature. The measure would raise the minimum purchase age from 18 to 21, require revised product labeling, prohibit the sale of synthetically derived kratom products, and classify products with high concentrations of 7-hydroxymitragynine as narcotic drugs under state law.

The bill draws a regulatory line between natural kratom leaf products and concentrated or synthetic 7-OH preparations. That distinction mirrors the framework the FDA has outlined in its own enforcement posture, where the agency has [said](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/public-health-focus/fda-and-kratom) it is targeting concentrated 7-OH products rather than natural leaf.

Arizona's approach contrasts with the outright bans that [Connecticut and Tennessee enacted last week][missouri-ag-kratom-probe-connecticut-tennessee-act] and the [full-scheduling pressure from the U.S. Senate][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down]. If signed into law, HB 2415 would join the regulatory model adopted by states like Utah and [New York][new-york-signs-kratom-age-limit-michigan-house-passes-ban], which have imposed age limits and product standards rather than blanket prohibitions.

## Delaware Weighs Controlled-Substance Classification

Delaware's General Assembly is considering a different path. A substitute version of [SB 262](https://legis.delaware.gov/BillDetail/143097) would define "kratom" and "kratom products" in state law, then prohibit their manufacture, distribution, sale, offer to sell, and possession with intent to sell, according to the bill text on the Delaware legislature's website. The bill would slot kratom into the state's existing controlled-substances offense structure.

The Rockefeller Institute of Government [noted](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/) in a recent analysis that the 2025-26 legislative cycle has produced prohibition-oriented bills in states including Delaware, Illinois, Michigan, South Dakota, and Tennessee, while consumer-protection-style bills have been introduced in states such as Hawaii, Idaho, Massachusetts, Ohio, and Pennsylvania.

## Federal Marshals Seize Kratom Products in California

The U.S. Attorney's Office for the Central District of California [announced](https://www.justice.gov/usao-cdca/pr/kratom-seized-california-united-states-marshals-service) that federal marshals seized more than 100 cases of products labeled as containing kratom. The products were alleged to be unapproved new drugs and misbranded drugs under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, according to the announcement.

The seizure adds to a pattern of federal enforcement that has intensified since the FDA [recommended](https://abc30.com/post/fda-pushes-ban-potent-kratom-derivative-7-amid-safety-concerns/17358223/) scheduling 7-OH as a controlled substance. The FDA maintains that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a drug, dietary supplement, or food additive in the United States.

## Minnesota Considers Kratom Restrictions

Minnesota lawmakers are weighing tighter restrictions on kratom, including raising the minimum purchase age to 21 or classifying the substance as Schedule II, [according to LiveNOW from FOX](https://www.livenowfox.com/news/kratom-poisonings-spikes-what-you-need-know-about-herbal-drug). The report cited rising kratom exposure calls as a factor in the legislative discussion.

A Schedule II classification would place kratom alongside substances with recognized medical use but high abuse potential, a categorization distinct from the Schedule I approach taken by [Kansas for 7-OH][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax] and by states that have banned kratom outright.
