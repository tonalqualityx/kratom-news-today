## California Moves to Regulate Kratom Products Under Existing Food and Drug Law

AB 1088, currently advancing through the California Legislature, would add kratom and 7-OH products to the Sherman Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Law, [according to bill tracking records](https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_202520260ab1088). The bill would impose alkaloid concentration limits on products sold in the state, require child-resistant packaging, and prohibit sales to anyone under 21.

The bill would place kratom within California's existing food and drug framework, applying manufacturing, labeling, and advertising standards already used for other consumer products. Governor Gavin Newsom [announced in March](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2026/03/03/governor-newsom-announces-95-compliance-with-prohibition-of-illegal-kratom-products/) that businesses had reached 95 percent compliance with the state's existing prohibition on illegal kratom products, and the California Department of Public Health (CDPH) had previously issued a statewide consumer warning on kratom and 7-OH products.

The California approach contrasts with the outright bans moving through legislatures in other states. [Kansas signed a 7-OH ban][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax] earlier this month, [Connecticut added kratom to its controlled-substance schedule][missouri-ag-kratom-probe-connecticut-tennessee-act], and [Delaware is weighing a full controlled-substance classification][arizona-kratom-bill-delaware-weighs-ban]. A [Rockefeller Institute of Government analysis](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/) published May 20 found that 29 states introduced kratom-related legislation during 2024 and 2025, with approaches splitting among outright bans, regulatory frameworks, and research-first mandates.

## North Carolina Introduces Licensing Framework With July 2026 Deadline

North Carolina's pending kratom regulation bill would require manufacturers and distributors to register with the state, comply with packaging and reporting rules, and meet advertising restrictions, [according to a summary from the state's School of Government Legislative Reporting Service](https://lrs.sog.unc.edu/bill/regulate-kratom-products). Licensing would be required by July 1, 2026.

The bill's structure follows the consumer-protection model that AKA has advocated at the state level, setting compliance standards for the industry. That approach differs from the scheduling path Connecticut and Tennessee took this month.

## AKA Schedules Congressional Briefing on 7-OH Enforcement for June 3

AKA announced a Congressional Briefing titled "FDA 7OH Action, Kratom, and Consumer Protection Legislation," [scheduled for June 3](https://www.protectkratom.org/congress). Mac Haddow, AKA's senior fellow, is listed as the point of contact.

The briefing comes amid an intensifying federal focus on concentrated 7-OH products. The [FDA announced](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers) that it is recommending the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) schedule certain 7-OH products under the Controlled Substances Act, issued a public report on 7-OH, sent a letter to health care professionals, and previously delivered warning letters to seven companies distributing 7-OH products. [FDA Commissioner Marty Makary told reporters](https://abc30.com/post/fda-pushes-ban-potent-kratom-derivative-7-amid-safety-concerns/17358223/), "Our focus is not on Kratom, our focus is on 7-OH," framing the agency's action as a targeted move against concentrated products rather than botanical kratom leaf.

AKA has historically advocated for the Kratom Consumer Protection Act model at the state level. In [February testimony before the Kansas Senate](https://kslegislature.gov/b2025_26/committees/testimony/pdf/?apn=b2025_26%2Fyear2%2Fsenate%2Fcommittees%2Fctte_s_phw_1%2Ftestimony%2Fpublished%2Fctte_s_phw_1_20260216_10_testimony.html), Haddow proposed a framework that would schedule products with 7-OH concentrations above 2 percent of total alkaloid content while keeping trace, naturally occurring 7-OH in leaf products legal. He argued that FDA Commissioner Makary and HHS Secretary Kennedy Jr. supported the distinction between synthetic 7-OH and natural kratom leaf.

The Global Kratom Coalition (GKC) has taken a parallel position, [stating](https://globalkratomcoalition.org/statement-on-7-hydroxymitragynine-products) that the safety of 7-OH products "has not been established" and that synthetically derived 7-OH "should not be considered leaf kratom products." GKC has also endorsed a federal bill by Rep. Gus Bilirakis that would amend the Controlled Substances Act to classify 7-OH as a Schedule I substance.

## Kratom Drinks Draw Research Scrutiny as Retail Presence Grows

A review [published in PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12799471/) this week argued that kratom-infused drinks are expanding in retail spaces "amid lax regulation and aggressive marketing." The authors called for federal oversight, preregistration of kratom drink products, standardized dosing disclosures, and mandatory warning labels.

A [2023 study published in PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10481347/) that surveyed California vape and smoke shops found kratom available in 62.4 percent of observed stores, with higher prevalence in locations that sold both vape and smoke products.

The review's authors noted that drink products may reach consumers who would not otherwise encounter kratom in capsule or powder form. California's AB 1088 would bring such products under state food and drug law if enacted.
