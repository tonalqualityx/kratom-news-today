## Missouri AG Adds 10 Entities, Widens Probe to Supply Chain

Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway [announced](https://ago.mo.gov/attorney-general-hanaway-expands-kratom-investigation-to-target-supply-chain/) an expansion of her office's kratom and 7-OH investigation, issuing Civil Investigative Demands (CIDs) to 10 additional entities across manufacturers, distributors, and retailers. The move brings the total number of entities under investigation to 22.

The expansion marks a shift in scope. Hanaway's [initial probe, announced last week][missouri-ag-kratom-probe-connecticut-tennessee-act], targeted six retailers and manufacturers including CBD Kratom and CBD American Shaman. The new round extends the investigation into the supply chain, according to the attorney general's office. CBD American Shaman and related entities were the subject of a lawsuit the office filed on March 31, 2026, the announcement noted.

Hanaway's office said it is using CIDs to examine businesses believed to be manufacturing, selling, or distributing kratom products that may violate Missouri law, and encouraged consumers who believe they were misled or harmed to file complaints with the state consumer protection office.

## Ohio Introduces Kratom Consumer Protection Act

Ohio Representatives Mike Odioso and Brian Lorenz introduced the Kratom Consumer Protection Act as [HB 587](https://ohiohouse.gov/news/republican/representatives-odioso-lorenz-introduce-kratom-consumer-protection-act-139385). The bill would require quality standards for kratom products sold in the state, license distributors and retailers, and ban synthetic kratom derivatives and products with concentrated 7-OH.

Odioso said Ohioans "deserve to know" that products are safe and sold in regulated venues, according to the legislative announcement. Lorenz said the bill would "keep Ohioans safe" by requiring quality standards and prohibiting synthetic kratom and 7-OH products.

The bill follows Ohio's [earlier ban on synthetic kratom compounds][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect], which took effect this month. HB 587 would create a regulatory framework for natural kratom products that remains legal in the state, setting the kind of consumer-protection standards that the American Kratom Association has promoted at the state level.

## Consumer Protection Bills Advance in Illinois and New Jersey

Illinois's [HB 1303](https://legiscan.com/IL/sponsors/HB1303/2025) would create a state Kratom Consumer Protection Act with age limits, product-composition restrictions, a ban on child-appealing packaging, federal-compliance requirements, and a retail tax. New Jersey's [S1384](https://www.njleg.state.nj.us/bill-search/2026/S1384/bill-text?f=S1500&n=1384_I1) would create a similar consumer-protection framework. Both bills are in committee.

The two bills add to a wave of consumer-protection legislation moving through state legislatures alongside the prohibition bills advancing in states like [Delaware][arizona-kratom-bill-delaware-weighs-ban] and Michigan. Delaware's [SB 262](https://legis.delaware.gov/BillDetail/143097), a substitute bill introduced this week, would make it unlawful to manufacture, distribute, sell, or offer to sell kratom products, while removing criminal charges for simple possession.

## GKC Endorses Federal Bill to Schedule 7-OH

The Global Kratom Coalition (GKC) [endorsed](https://globalkratomcoalition.org/kratom-news) a federal bill introduced by Rep. Gus Bilirakis that would amend the Controlled Substances Act to classify 7-hydroxymitragynine as a Schedule I substance. GKC has [separately stated](https://globalkratomcoalition.org/statement-on-7-hydroxymitragynine-products) that the safety of concentrated 7-OH products "has not been established" and that synthetically derived 7-OH "should not be considered leaf kratom products."

The endorsement aligns with the [FDA's position](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers) that its enforcement action targets concentrated 7-OH products rather than natural kratom leaf. The FDA has recommended that the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) schedule certain 7-OH products under the Controlled Substances Act. [California's AB 1088][california-kratom-sales-bill-north-carolina-licensing], which would impose alkaloid concentration caps on kratom products sold in the state, takes a different regulatory approach to the same underlying concern about concentrated compounds.
