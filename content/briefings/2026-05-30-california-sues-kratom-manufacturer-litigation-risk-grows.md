California's Department of Public Health (CDPH) filed a legal action against Ashlynn Marketing Group, a Southern California manufacturer, for allegedly continuing to produce and distribute kratom-containing food products after state embargoes. A separate legal analysis warned that kratom businesses across the supply chain face growing litigation exposure as enforcement accelerates.

## California Targets Manufacturer Over Embargo Violations

CDPH alleged that Ashlynn Marketing Group continued manufacturing and selling kratom-containing products even after the state issued embargoes and warnings, [according to the Sacramento Bee](https://www.sacbee.com/news/politics-government/capitol-alert/article315603609.html). The state also alleged that embargoed materials were moved without authorization.

California public health officials said kratom-derived products have been linked to "addiction, serious health harms, overdose and death," the Bee reported. The state is seeking to stop sales, seize and destroy inventory, and impose civil penalties.

CDPH said it has seized more than $5 million in kratom products across California as part of its broader enforcement effort. The action follows a [federal marshals seizure of more than 100 cases of kratom products in the state][arizona-kratom-bill-delaware-weighs-ban] earlier this week.

The FDA maintains that kratom is not lawfully marketed in the U.S. as a drug, dietary supplement, or food additive, [according to the agency's kratom information page](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/public-health-focus/fda-and-kratom).

## Legal Analysis Flags Escalating Litigation Risk

A legal analysis published by [JD Supra](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/the-emerging-kratom-litigation-1745315/) warned that manufacturers, distributors, and retailers of kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products face escalating litigation risk. The analysis cited growing FDA scrutiny and state enforcement actions, including actions targeting retailers selling products containing synthetic alkaloids.

The analysis described the litigation landscape as affecting every level of the supply chain. That framing mirrors the approach taken by Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway, who [expanded her statewide kratom investigation to 22 entities][missouri-ag-expands-kratom-probe-ohio-consumer-protection-act] this week, issuing Civil Investigative Demands to manufacturers, distributors, and retailers.

Missouri's probe has already produced its first lawsuit: Hanaway's office filed suit against CBD American Shaman and related entities on March 31, 2026, over the manufacture, distribution, and retail sale of kratom and its alkaloids, according to the AG's office.

## Florida Court Challenge to 7-OH Ban

Separately, [CBS Miami reported](https://www.cbsnews.com/miami/tag/kratom/) that a court challenge has been filed in Florida against a ban on the kratom compound 7-OH. Details of the challenge were limited, but it arrives as multiple states continue to restrict concentrated 7-OH products through legislation and enforcement.

The pattern of legal challenges to state kratom restrictions is likely to grow as more states enact bans and regulatory frameworks, the JD Supra analysis suggested. At least 30 states and the District of Columbia now regulate kratom in some form, according to a January 2026 summary by the Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association (LAPPA).
