## White House Strategy Includes Kratom in Overdose Data

The White House's [2026 National Drug Control Strategy](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/National-Drug-Control-Strategy-2026-1.pdf), published this month by the Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP), cited overdose deaths in which kratom or its primary alkaloid mitragynine was detected. The inclusion places kratom alongside fentanyl, methamphetamine, and other substances tracked in the federal government's annual drug mortality assessment.

The strategy did not propose kratom-specific enforcement action. The framing is consistent with the FDA's existing position that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a drug, dietary supplement, or food additive in the United States, and that its use carries risks including liver toxicity, seizures, and substance use disorder, [according to the agency's kratom page](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/public-health-focus/fda-and-kratom).

The document's release adds another layer to the federal posture on kratom. The FDA recommended that the DEA schedule synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) preparations earlier this month, and multiple members of Congress have introduced bills targeting concentrated 7-OH products.

## Massachusetts Towns Enact Local Bans as State Bills Stall

In Massachusetts, municipal boards of health are acting on kratom restrictions while statewide legislation remains in committee, [according to South Shore News](https://www.southshore.news/p/the-kratom-crisis-deepens-a-divided). Rockland's Board of Health unanimously enacted an immediate ban on all forms of kratom on May 6. Hanover's Board of Health is scheduled to vote on a total prohibition on June 2.

The local actions reflect a pattern that has emerged in other states: when legislatures are slow to act, local governments fill the gap. Washington state saw a [similar dynamic earlier this year][washington-local-governments-fill-kratom-regulatory-vacuum], with county health boards and city councils adopting or considering kratom restrictions while statewide bills remained under debate.

Massachusetts has both consumer protection and prohibition bills pending at the state level. The board-of-health approach allows individual communities to restrict sales without waiting for the legislature, though it creates the kind of jurisdictional patchwork that industry groups have argued makes compliance difficult for multi-state operators.

## Poison Center Exposures Rise Sharply, but Causation Questions Persist

A Medical College of Wisconsin analysis reported that kratom exposures reported to U.S. poison centers rose from 19 in 2010 to 1,242 in 2023, [according to MCW's newsroom](https://www.mcw.edu/newsroom/news-articles/kratom-use-is-surging-in-the-us-with-life-changing-consequences). Severe outcomes climbed to 158 cases in 2023. Separately, a CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report published in March documented an approximately [1,200 percent increase](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7511a1.htm) in kratom-related poison center calls from 2015 to 2025, with multi-substance exposures accounting for a significant share of the most severe cases.

The poison center data has been cited by regulators and state lawmakers as evidence of growing public health concern. The MCW data and the CDC report both document rising exposure counts, though they use different time windows and data sources.

A 2025 case-review published in [Frontiers in Pharmacology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2025.1620601/full) offered a counterpoint on causation. The review found insufficient evidence to support the claim that kratom consumption alone increases severe acute adverse effects, and emphasized that confounding substances were present in many reported cases. The authors noted that polypharmacy complicates the attribution of harm to kratom specifically.

The gap between rising exposure numbers and disputed causation continues to shape the policy conversation. Groups including the American Kratom Association and the Global Kratom Coalition have argued that distinguishing between natural leaf products and concentrated or synthetic 7-OH products is essential to sound policy. Federal and state regulators have increasingly focused enforcement on 7-OH products while leaving natural leaf kratom in a less defined regulatory position.
