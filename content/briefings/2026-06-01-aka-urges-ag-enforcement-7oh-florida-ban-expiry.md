The American Kratom Association called on state attorneys general to enforce against concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products, issuing a policy advisory that draws a sharp line between natural kratom leaf and what it describes as "chemically manipulated" 7-OH preparations. Florida's emergency scheduling order covering concentrated 7-OH approaches its June 30 expiry without a clear legislative replacement, and Rhode Island has moved in the opposite direction, reversing its kratom ban in favor of regulated sales with an excise tax.

## AKA Draws Line on 7-OH Enforcement

The American Kratom Association (AKA) issued a policy advisory urging state attorneys general to take "immediate enforcement action" against 7-OH products sold in states that have passed Kratom Consumer Protection Acts, according to [Big Technology](https://www.bigtechnology.com/p/american-kratom-association-issues). Mac Haddow, the organization's senior adviser, said "7-OH is not kratom" and described concentrated 7-OH products as chemically manipulated rather than natural leaf preparations.

The advisory calls for enforcement specifically in states where consumer protection frameworks already distinguish between natural kratom leaf and synthetic or concentrated derivatives. Haddow's statement argued that trace, naturally occurring 7-OH in leaf kratom should remain protected, while concentrated or synthetic 7-OH should be targeted by regulators.

The advisory aligns with positions taken by the Global Kratom Coalition (GKC), which has separately endorsed a federal bill introduced by Rep. Gus Bilirakis to classify synthetic 7-OH as a Schedule I substance. GKC has attributed the recent spike in poison center calls to concentrated 7-OH products, [arguing](https://globalkratomcoalition.org/poison-center-7oh) that kratom-labeled calls were relatively flat from 2018 through 2024 before rising sharply in 2025. The CDC's most recent data showed kratom-related poison center reports reached 3,434 in 2025, an approximately 1,200 percent increase over the past decade, though the agency's report noted that multi-substance exposures accounted for a significant share of severe outcomes.

The AKA advisory adds pressure to the [ongoing wave of state attorney general enforcement][missouri-ag-expands-kratom-probe-ohio-consumer-protection-act] that has expanded in recent weeks. Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway has extended her investigation to 22 entities across the kratom supply chain, and Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall issued a statewide cease-and-desist order targeting products containing mitragynine and 7-OH, according to the [Alabama Attorney General's office](https://www.alabamaag.gov/alabama-attorney-general-issues-statewide-cease-and-desist-order-targeting-illegal-kratom-products/).

## Florida's 7-OH Emergency Order Nears June 30 Deadline

Florida's emergency scheduling order covering concentrated 7-OH, issued in August 2025 after state officials cited increased emergency room visits and adverse incidents among people under 25, is set to expire on June 30 unless extended or replaced by legislative action, according to a [Rockefeller Institute of Government policy analysis](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/). The Florida legislature has advanced bills to tighten kratom labeling, testing, and processing requirements, [according to WFTV](https://www.wftv.com/news/local/kratom-crackdown-florida-lawmakers-aim-tighten-regulations-families-warn-deadly-risks/J5EQLBZKOVG67NAYD6FKB3Z7TE/), but no permanent replacement for the emergency order has been signed into law.

The expiry creates a regulatory gap for Florida's kratom market. If the emergency order lapses without a replacement, concentrated 7-OH products could return to legal sale in the state at a time when the [broader enforcement environment][california-sues-kratom-manufacturer-litigation-risk-grows] is moving in the restrictive direction.

## Rhode Island Reverses Ban, Adds Excise Tax

Rhode Island has reversed its longstanding kratom ban and moved to regulated sales with an excise tax, according to the [Rockefeller Institute analysis](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/). The shift makes Rhode Island one of the few states to move from prohibition to a regulated market framework, a direction that contrasts with recent bans in Connecticut and scheduling actions in Kansas.

The Rockefeller Institute described the broader state policy landscape as moving in "opposite directions," with some states pursuing outright bans or scheduling while others adopt consumer protection frameworks with age restrictions, labeling requirements, and adulteration controls. A summary by the Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association found that 29 states introduced kratom-related legislation in 2024 and 2025, with 22 of those pursuing regulation of possession, distribution, sale, or manufacture rather than prohibition.

## Washington State Weighs Consumer Protection Bill

Washington's House Consumer Protection and Business committee has scheduled a public hearing on [HB 2291](https://app.leg.wa.gov/BillSummary/?BillNumber=2291&Year=2026), a bill that would establish a Kratom Consumer Protection Act in the state. The bill was prefiled and referred to committee. Details of the bill's specific provisions, including any age restrictions or product standards, were not immediately available from the legislative summary.

Washington would join a growing list of states considering regulatory frameworks as an alternative to prohibition, following models established in states like Utah, Nevada, and Georgia.
