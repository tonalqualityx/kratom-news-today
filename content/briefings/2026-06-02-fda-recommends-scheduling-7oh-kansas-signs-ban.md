The FDA recommended that the DEA place concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products on the federal controlled substances schedule while drawing an explicit line between those products and natural kratom leaf. Commissioner Marty Makary described 7-OH tablets, gummies, and shots as a distinct category from kratom, and the agency issued warning letters to seven companies, sent alerts to physicians, and published a new report on 7-OH health risks. Separately, Kansas signed 7-OH substances into Schedule I, and a CDC analysis reported a roughly 1,200 percent rise in kratom-related poison center calls over the past decade.

## FDA Recommends DEA Schedule Concentrated 7-OH

The FDA [announced](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers) that it is sending a letter to the DEA recommending that concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine products be placed under the Controlled Substances Act. The agency said it also issued warning letters in June to seven companies distributing 7-OH products, including tablets, gummies, drink mixes, and shots. The FDA sent a "Dear Doctor" letter to health care professionals and published a consumer warning about 7-OH.

Commissioner Makary said the agency is pursuing a "concentration threshold" approach to distinguish concentrated 7-OH products from the trace amounts of 7-hydroxymitragynine that occur naturally in kratom leaf, [according to a press briefing](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SMbMkVQOHGQ). He also said the FDA has "no evidence" that kratom is safe or effective for any medical use, a position consistent with the agency's [standing kratom page](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/public-health-focus/fda-and-kratom).

The scheduling recommendation now goes to the DEA, which holds final scheduling authority through its own rulemaking and public comment process. The DEA has not announced any new kratom-specific action. Its most recent publicly stated position is a fact sheet describing kratom's alkaloids and their effects, last updated after the agency's 2016 scheduling attempt was [withdrawn following public backlash](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2016/10/13/2016-24659/withdrawal-of-notice-of-intent-to-temporarily-place-mitragynine-and-7-hydroxymitragynine-into).

The American Kratom Association (AKA) [applauded](https://www.americankratom.org/news/aka-response-to-fda) the recommendation, calling it a "science-driven" move. The AKA specifically praised HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and Commissioner Makary for targeting concentrated 7-OH while preserving access to natural kratom leaf products. The position aligns with the AKA's recent [policy advisory to state attorneys general][aka-urges-ag-enforcement-7oh-florida-ban-expiry] urging enforcement against 7-OH products sold as kratom.

The Global Kratom Coalition (GKC) has separately endorsed a federal bill introduced by Rep. Gus Bilirakis that would classify synthetic 7-OH as a Schedule I substance. GKC has argued that synthetically derived 7-hydroxymitragynine should not be treated as a leaf kratom product.

## Kansas Signs 7-OH Into Schedule I

Kansas Governor Laura Kelly signed House Bill 2365, placing 7-OH-related substances in Schedule I, [according to the governor's office](https://www.governor.ks.gov/Home/Components/News/News/1016/55). Kelly said the state should "safeguard the health of Kansans" until scientific reviews demonstrate positive benefits and minimal risks.

The signing follows an earlier round of [state-level scheduling actions][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax] and adds Kansas to a growing list of states that have moved to restrict 7-OH independently of federal action. A [Rockefeller Institute of Government analysis](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/) found that states are moving in three directions: outright bans, controlled substance scheduling, and regulated sales frameworks with consumer protection requirements.

## Arizona AG Warns on 7-OH; Idaho Advances Consumer Protection

Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes issued a [consumer alert](https://www.azag.gov/ag-mayes-consumer-alert-kratom) warning that products sold as kratom or "all-natural" supplements may contain concentrated synthetic substances, including 7-hydroxymitragynine. Mayes said she plans to work with lawmakers to strengthen Arizona's kratom laws and urged consumers to avoid mislabeled or "extra strength" products lacking clear ingredient and alkaloid disclosures.

In Idaho, [Senate Bill 1418](https://idahofreedom.org/senate-bill-1418-kratom-consumer-protection/) would create a Kratom Consumer Protection and Safety Act that prohibits adulterated kratom products, sets a 21-and-older age limit, allows random and unannounced inspections, and adds misdemeanor penalties for knowingly misrepresenting age. The bill is advancing through the legislature.

## CDC Reports 1,200 Percent Rise in Kratom Poison Center Calls

A CDC analysis published in the [Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7511a1.htm) found that kratom-related reports to U.S. poison centers rose approximately 1,200 percent from 2015 to 2025, with 2025 setting a record. The report found that severe outcomes were especially associated with multiple-substance exposures, and that about half of cases involving multiple substances required hospitalization.

The CDC data does not distinguish between exposures involving natural kratom leaf and those involving concentrated 7-OH products, a distinction that the FDA's new concentration-threshold approach is designed to address. The AKA and GKC have both argued that the spike in adverse reports is driven primarily by concentrated 7-OH products rather than by traditional kratom leaf.
