## Mayer Brown Flags Growing Litigation Exposure for Kratom Companies

Law firm Mayer Brown published a [legal analysis](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/05/the-emerging-kratom-litigation-landscape-and-implications-for-similarly-situated-manufacturers) in late May warning that manufacturers, retailers, and distributors of kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products face a "growing" combination of FDA enforcement, state enforcement, and product-liability risk. The analysis noted that wrongful-death claims, failure-to-warn suits, and allegations of deceptive marketing are increasing across the industry.

The Mayer Brown report pointed to the FDA's recent actions, including warning letters issued to seven companies distributing concentrated 7-OH products and the agency's [recommendation that the DEA schedule concentrated 7-OH][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] under the Controlled Substances Act. The analysis suggested that the FDA's enforcement posture, combined with state attorney general investigations and a Tampa Bay Times [investigation](https://project.tampabay.com/investigations/deadly-dose/kratom-industry-opms-supply-chain-indonesia-florida/) documenting opaque supply chain structures in the kratom industry, could accelerate the litigation pipeline.

Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall's statewide [cease-and-desist order](https://www.alabamaag.gov/alabama-attorney-general-issues-statewide-cease-and-desist-order-targeting-illegal-kratom-products/) targeting businesses selling products containing mitragynine and 7-OH, and the [California lawsuit against a kratom manufacturer][california-sues-kratom-manufacturer-litigation-risk-grows] filed last week, illustrate the enforcement climate the Mayer Brown analysis described. The FDA [maintains](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/public-health-focus/fda-and-kratom) that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a dietary supplement or food additive in the United States.

## South Dakota Panel Advances Kratom Ban Bills

A South Dakota Senate panel advanced bills that would ban kratom in the state, sending them to the full Senate for a floor vote, [News from the States reported](https://www.newsfromthestates.com/article/panel-sends-bills-ban-kratom-and-hemp-derived-consumables-senate-floor). The panel did not formally endorse the legislation but moved the bills forward. The legislation would prohibit the sale and distribution of kratom if passed by the full chamber and signed by the governor.

South Dakota's approach falls at the prohibition end of the state regulatory spectrum. The bills advanced alongside proposals to restrict hemp-derived consumables, linking the two product categories in the same legislative push. The American Kratom Association has previously opposed outright bans, arguing that consumer-protection frameworks with age limits and product standards are a more effective regulatory model than prohibition.

## Minnesota Weighs Schedule II Classification

Minnesota lawmakers are considering classifying kratom as a Schedule II controlled substance and raising the minimum purchase age to 21, [FOX 9 reported](https://www.livenowfox.com/news/kratom-poisonings-spikes-what-you-need-know-about-herbal-drug). A Schedule II classification would place kratom in the same regulatory tier as drugs that carry an accepted medical use with severe restrictions, a significantly more restrictive approach than the consumer-protection frameworks adopted in states like Utah and Nevada.

The Minnesota proposal surfaced amid coverage of the CDC's recent finding that kratom-related poison center calls rose approximately 1,200 percent over the past decade. Minnesota currently has no statewide kratom regulation. The AKA and the Global Kratom Coalition have both argued that the spike in adverse reports is driven primarily by concentrated 7-OH products rather than by traditional kratom leaf, a distinction that a Schedule II classification of kratom broadly would not preserve.

## New York Senate Bill Would Go Beyond Hochul's Signed Laws

New York Senate Bill [S7379](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/S7379) would schedule kratom as a controlled substance in New York, taking a more restrictive approach than the [age-limit and warning-label bills][new-york-signs-kratom-age-limit-michigan-house-passes-ban] Governor Kathy Hochul signed in late May. Those two laws restricted kratom sales to adults 21 and older and required warning labels statewide.

S7379 would prohibit kratom sales and possession entirely if enacted. The bill is active in the Senate. The introduction of a scheduling bill weeks after the governor signed the less restrictive consumer-protection approach reflects the ongoing disagreement among New York lawmakers over whether regulation or prohibition is the appropriate response to kratom's growing presence in retail channels.
