## Federal government drops Botanic Tonics case

The federal government dismissed its enforcement case against Botanic Tonics LLC, the manufacturer of Feel Free kratom-based beverages, [according to Kratom Anonymous](https://www.kratomanonymous.com/news). The case had been one of the FDA's higher-profile enforcement actions against a branded kratom product line, and the dismissal drew attention from industry observers.

Botanic Tonics had previously challenged the Utah Kratom Regulation Act alongside the Global Kratom Coalition in federal court. The company's Feel Free product, a liquid kratom shot marketed through convenience and wellness channels, drew FDA attention for marketing claims and product formulation.

The dismissal arrives as the [FDA continues to push for DEA scheduling of concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine products][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban]. The agency has increasingly distinguished between concentrated 7-OH preparations and leaf-based kratom products in its recent enforcement activity, though it has not publicly commented on the reasons for the Botanic Tonics dismissal.

## New York signs kratom sales restrictions and labeling requirements

New York Governor Kathy Hochul signed two pieces of legislation restricting kratom sales and labeling in the state, [according to the governor's office](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-signs-two-pieces-landmark-legislation-protect-new-yorkers-against-harmful).

The first law prohibits the sale of kratom to individuals under 21 and sets civil penalties of up to $500 per violation. The second requires manufacturers, distributors, and retailers to include consumer warning labels and full ingredient lists on all kratom products sold in New York. The labels must warn that the product may be addictive and may interact with certain medications and controlled substances.

The governor's office described the measures as a public-health response to what it called the "harmful effects of kratom." The statement framed the restrictions as regulating a product "sold for recreational use."

A separate bill pending in the New York State Senate, [S7379](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/S7379), would go further by classifying kratom as a Schedule I controlled substance under state law. The sponsor memo states the bill's purpose is "to prevent the sale and use of kratom." That bill has not passed either chamber.

Several states have adopted similar age-and-labeling frameworks in 2026 while separate scheduling proposals advance through other legislative channels. At the federal level, a [bipartisan Senate letter urged the FDA to schedule all kratom][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down], pushing beyond the agency's current focus on concentrated 7-OH products.

## Alabama AG issues statewide cease-and-desist on kratom products

Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall issued a statewide cease-and-desist order targeting businesses selling products containing mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine, [according to the attorney general's office](https://www.alabamaag.gov/alabama-attorney-general-issues-statewide-cease-and-desist-order-targeting-illegal-kratom-products/). Both alkaloids are classified as Schedule I controlled substances under Alabama's Controlled Substances Act, according to the attorney general's office.

The office stated that lab testing confirmed the presence of these substances in products on store shelves. The order covers all retail businesses in the state and the office said it is coordinating with local prosecutors and law enforcement on compliance.

Alabama is one of a small number of states whose controlled-substances schedules include kratom's primary alkaloids. The attorney general's office described the cease-and-desist as enforcement of existing state law.

## Missouri AG expands kratom investigation

Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway issued six Civil Investigative Demands to kratom manufacturers, distributors, and retailers as part of a statewide investigation, [according to the attorney general's office](https://ago.mo.gov/attorney-general-hanaway-launches-investigation-into-major-kratom-manufacturers-and-retailers-for-potentially-dangerous-and-illegal-practices/). The office said it is concerned that consumers may be exposed to "unapproved, unsafe, and deceptively marketed opioid-like drugs."

The investigation expands on earlier enforcement activity. The [New Hampshire House passed legislation targeting enhanced 7-OH products][new-hampshire-kratom-bill-missouri-ag-expands-probe] the same week Hanaway announced the initial probe in May, and her office has since broadened the scope to encompass the full supply chain.

## Connecticut kratom scheduling now in effect

Connecticut approved regulations designating Mitragyna speciosa (kratom) and 7-hydroxymitragynine as Schedule I controlled substances, effective April 2, 2026, [according to the Connecticut Department of Consumer Protection](https://portal.ct.gov/dcp/knowledge-base/articles/drug-control/patients/kratom-update-october-2025). The state's approach classifies both the plant material and the concentrated alkaloid under the same scheduling framework.

Connecticut joins Alabama and a handful of other states that have placed kratom itself (rather than only concentrated 7-OH products) into their controlled-substances schedules. The approach contrasts with states like New York and Utah, which have opted for sales restrictions and product-safety regulation while keeping the plant legal for adults.

## Washington kratom bill fails

Washington lawmakers considered a kratom consumer-protection bill during the 2026 legislative session that would have restricted kratom use to adults 21 and older and regulated production and retail in a framework similar to cannabis-style oversight, [according to MRSC](https://mrsc.org/stay-informed/mrsc-insight/may-2026/kratom-bans). The bill did not pass.

The failure leaves Washington without a state-level kratom regulatory framework. Several municipalities in the state have enacted local restrictions independently.

## What to watch

New York's pending Schedule I bill (S7379) would move the state from sales restrictions to a full ban if passed. Alabama's cease-and-desist sets up enforcement actions that could produce case law on kratom product seizures. The FDA has increasingly distinguished between concentrated 7-OH products and leaf-based kratom in its [recent scheduling recommendation][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban], and the Botanic Tonics dismissal will be watched for whether it reflects that narrowing focus.
