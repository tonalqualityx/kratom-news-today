Rhode Island's Department of Health approved the state's first kratom manufacturer license, marking an early test of the state's regulatory framework months after legalization. In Missouri, a different kind of state action: Shaman Botanicals, the parent company of CBD American Shaman, agreed to suspend all kratom sales in the state following allegations of deceptive marketing.

## Rhode Island Operationalizes Kratom Licensing

The Rhode Island Department of Health issued the state's first kratom manufacturer license to an out-of-state company, [WJAR reported on June 5](https://turnto10.com/news/local/rhode-island-department-of-health-approves-first-kratom-manufacturer-license-legalized-applications-pending-retailer-distributor-importer-june-5-2026). The license is the first issued under Rhode Island's kratom legalization law, which took effect earlier this year and created licensing categories for manufacturers, retailers, distributors, and importers.

The department said it has received 20 applications across the four categories, with 19 still pending. Under the law, kratom products must be kept behind store counters, labeled as potentially habit-forming, and sold only to buyers 21 or older. The law prohibits kratom in food or drinks, bans packaging or advertising that appeals to minors, and prohibits the synthetic concentrate 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH). Unlicensed sales carry fines.

The license issuance follows this week's reintroduction of an [expanded kratom regulation bill in the Rhode Island legislature][rhode-island-kratom-bill-missouri-ag-california-regulation], which grew from six pages to 25 with input from the state health department. Rhode Island's approach places the state among a small group testing whether a licensing and regulation model can coexist with the stricter prohibitions adopted elsewhere.

The synthetic concentrate 7-OH remains legal in neighboring Massachusetts, where local boards of health have been enacting municipal bans as statewide legislation stalls.

## American Shaman Suspends Missouri Kratom Sales

Shaman Botanicals, LLC, operating as CBD American Shaman, agreed to suspend all in-state kratom sales in Missouri, effective immediately, [KCTV5 reported on June 4](https://www.kctv5.com/2026/06/04/american-shaman-agrees-suspend-kratom-sales-missouri/). The suspension covers kratom products and alkaloids (particularly 7-OH) sold to Missouri consumers through both retail stores and online channels.

Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway alleged that American Shaman engaged in deceptive marketing practices, including advertising "free samples" of 7-OH without disclosing addiction risks and targeting consumers struggling with addiction. "Kratom alkaloids, especially 7-OH, are dangerous opioids that carry serious health risks," Hanaway said, according to KCTV5.

Under the agreement, the company has 90 days to remove all kratom products from Missouri locations and 30 days to halt Missouri-targeted advertising, including billboards. The suspension does not constitute a full statewide ban, and KCTV5 reported that Hanaway indicated intentions to lobby the legislature for prohibition.

The suspension is the most significant enforcement outcome to date from [Hanaway's expanding kratom investigation][missouri-ag-kratom-probe-connecticut-tennessee-act], which now covers [22 entities across the supply chain][rhode-island-kratom-bill-missouri-ag-california-regulation]. Hanaway's office launched the investigation in November 2025, filed suit against American Shaman in March, and expanded the probe to 10 additional entities in the supply chain [earlier this week](https://ago.mo.gov/attorney-general-hanaway-expands-kratom-investigation-to-target-supply-chain/).

## Tennessee Kratom Ban Takes Effect July 1

Tennessee's kratom prohibition, known as Matthew's Law, [takes effect July 1](https://www.wksr.com/news/local-news/kratom-law-goes-into-effect-7-1/), according to WKSR. The law bans the sale and possession of kratom products statewide. Tennessee [passed the ban in late May][missouri-ag-kratom-probe-connecticut-tennessee-act] after weeks of legislative debate.

The effective date places Tennessee alongside Kansas, where a [7-OH scheduling bill signed by Governor Laura Kelly][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] also takes effect July 1. Kansas's penalties for 7-OH possession will be equivalent to those for heroin possession, according to state officials.

The two July 1 effective dates will test enforcement capacity in both states. Tennessee's law covers all kratom products, while Kansas targeted concentrated 7-OH specifically, leaving natural kratom leaf products legal under state law.
