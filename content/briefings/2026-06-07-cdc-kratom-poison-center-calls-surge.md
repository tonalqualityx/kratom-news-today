A CDC report published this week found that U.S. poison centers received 3,434 kratom-related exposure reports in 2025, roughly 1,200 percent more than a decade earlier. Cases involving kratom combined with other substances showed worse clinical outcomes. The data adds a public-health dimension to the federal regulatory debate over kratom and its concentrated derivative, 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH).

## CDC Poison Center Data Shows Decade of Growth

The CDC's Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) [published data on June 5](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/75/wr/mm7511a1.htm) showing that kratom-related calls to U.S. poison centers reached 3,434 in 2025, compared with roughly 263 in 2015. The report described the increase as approximately 1,200 percent over the decade.

The MMWR found that exposures involving kratom combined with other substances produced more severe outcomes than kratom-alone exposures. The report did not break down the data by product type, meaning the figures include both natural kratom leaf products and concentrated preparations containing 7-OH, the kratom-derived alkaloid now under [federal scheduling review][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban].

[UVA Health summarized the findings](https://news.med.virginia.edu/research/kratom-calls-skyrocket-to-nations-poison-centers/) on the same day, noting the sharp rise and the multi-substance finding. The [Medical College of Wisconsin](https://www.mcw.edu/newsroom/news-articles/kratom-use-is-surging-in-the-us-with-life-changing-consequences) published a separate summary describing rising kratom exposure data and severe outcomes in poison center reporting.

## Context: Product Distinctions in the Data

The MMWR report arrives at a moment when federal regulators and researchers are drawing sharper lines between different kratom product categories. The FDA [announced earlier this month](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers) that it would recommend DEA scheduling of concentrated 7-OH products while explicitly stating that the action does not target natural kratom leaf.

[Johns Hopkins researchers published clinical guidance](https://www.hopkinsmedicine.org/news/articles/2025/02/emerging-use-of-kratom-in-us-kratom-use-disorder-experts-propose-decision-making-algorithm-for-clinicians) in February 2025 urging clinicians to distinguish among whole-leaf kratom, extracts, blended products, and kratom-derived synthetic products. The Hopkins team noted that 7-OH products may carry clinical profiles closer to opioid use disorder than typical kratom leaf use.

The CDC data does not reflect that distinction. Poison center reporting captures all kratom-related calls under a single category, regardless of whether the exposure involved whole-leaf products, extracts, or concentrated 7-OH preparations. The American Kratom Association has argued that conflating product types overstates the risk profile of natural kratom leaf. The FDA has maintained that kratom in all forms lacks FDA approval and has issued warnings about adverse events associated with kratom products.

The MMWR publication lands amid active state-level legislative debates over kratom. Georgia [signed kratom restrictions into law this week][georgia-kratom-law-connecticut-schedule-one], and multiple states face July 1 deadlines for kratom laws taking effect.
