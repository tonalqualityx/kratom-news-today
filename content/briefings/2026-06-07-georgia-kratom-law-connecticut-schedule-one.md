Georgia's governor signed HB 181 into law, adding 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) to the state's kratom definition and restricting sales to adults 21 and older. The law takes effect July 1, the same date as Tennessee's full kratom ban. In Connecticut, retailers began removing kratom products from shelves after the state classified kratom as a Schedule I controlled substance.

## Georgia Signs Kratom Restrictions into Law

Georgia's governor signed HB 181 after the state Senate passed the measure 23 to 3, according to [Georgia General Assembly records](https://www.legis.ga.gov/api/document/docs/default-source/house-budget-and-research-office-document-library/newsandhighlights/2024/2024_end_of_session_report_key_legislation.pdf?sfvrsn=cbd41d11_2). The law adds 7-hydroxymitragynine to the statutory definition of "kratom," restricts sales to buyers 21 and older, bans heated or vaporized delivery methods, and requires kratom to be sold behind the counter or in a secured display. Violations carry misdemeanor penalties.

The law takes effect July 1, 2026. That date now carries three state-level kratom deadlines: Georgia's restrictions, Tennessee's full prohibition under [Matthew's Law][rhode-island-first-kratom-license-american-shaman-missouri-suspension], and Kansas's 7-OH scheduling law signed [earlier this month][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban]. The three states took different approaches: Tennessee banned all kratom products, Kansas targeted concentrated 7-OH specifically while leaving natural leaf legal, and Georgia opted for age-gated, display-restricted regulation that includes 7-OH in its kratom definition.

Georgia's approach places it among a growing number of states choosing regulation over prohibition. A [Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association summary](https://legislativeanalysis.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Kratom-Summary-of-State-Laws.pdf) from April 2025 found that 29 states introduced kratom-related legislation during 2024 and 2025, with most proposals focused on regulating possession, distribution, sale, or manufacture rather than banning kratom outright. Several states had what the association described as "dueling" bills, where one set of lawmakers sought to schedule kratom components while another pursued consumer-protection regulation.

## Connecticut Pulls Kratom from Shelves After Schedule I Classification

Connecticut retailers began pulling kratom products from store shelves after the state classified kratom as a Schedule I controlled substance, the [Lakeville Journal reported](https://lakevillejournal.com/kratom-an-herbal-drug-pulled-from-northwest-corner-shelves-after-state-ban). State regulators cited concerns about unregulated sales and youth access as the basis for the classification, the Lakeville Journal reported.

Under Connecticut law, Schedule I is the state's most restrictive controlled substance classification, placing kratom in the same category as heroin and LSD. The classification prohibits commercial sale and possession.

Connecticut's classification stands in contrast to states that have chosen regulation over prohibition. Rhode Island issued its [first kratom manufacturer license][rhode-island-first-kratom-license-american-shaman-missouri-suspension] this week under a new licensing framework, while Washington's legislature has been [hearing HB 2291](https://wacities.org/news/2026/02/02/house-hears-bill-to-create-kratom-regulation-framework--with-preemption-measures), a bill that would create a statewide kratom regulatory framework with preemption measures and an 11% excise tax on retail sales.

The result is a state-by-state patchwork, with each state adopting its own position on whether kratom should be banned, age-gated, licensed, taxed, or left unregulated.
