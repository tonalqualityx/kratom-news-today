The Idaho Falls City Council voted unanimously last week to ban kratom sales within city limits, effective July 1, after the Bonneville County coroner linked the substance to six deaths over 18 months. The ban makes Idaho Falls the first city in the state to prohibit kratom sales outright. Separately, the parents of a 32-year-old Palm Beach County man who died in April 2025 filed a wrongful death lawsuit against Botanic Tonics and five Florida retailers, alleging the company's Feel Free beverage was marketed as a wellness product while concealing serious health risks.

## Idaho Falls passes unanimous kratom ban

The Idaho Falls City Council approved the sales ban on June 5, according to [Idaho News](https://idahonews.com/news/local/idaho-falls-bans-kratom-sales-starting-july-1-after-coroner-links-it-to-6-deaths) and [Local News 8](https://localnews8.com/news/2026/06/05/ban-of-kratom-sales-passed-unaminously-in-idaho-falls/). The vote was unanimous.

The action followed an October 2025 statement from Bonneville County Coroner Shante Sanchez, who said her office had confirmed six kratom-related deaths in the preceding 18 months. Sanchez described the findings as an urgent public health warning and told [KTVB](https://www.ktvb.com/article/news/health/city-of-idaho-falls-bans-sale-of-kratom/277-4072d01f-63ce-4805-81c9-cf66332c38a1) that kratom was "implicated in approximately 18 percent of the drug overdose fatalities in our county over the last year and a half." Victims ranged in age from 19 to 42.

The council had been [weighing a ban since at least May][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh], when the proposal first drew public attention alongside state-level discussions about kratom regulation. Prior attempts to restrict kratom at the Idaho state legislative level in 2026 did not advance, according to Idaho News, leaving local governments to act on their own.

The July 1 effective date aligns with bans taking effect the same day in Georgia and Tennessee, adding to a growing patchwork of state and local restrictions across the country.

## Florida family sues Botanic Tonics and retailers over Feel Free death

In a separate development, the parents of Kevin Oliveira filed a wrongful death lawsuit against Botanic Tonics, LLC, the manufacturer of the Feel Free Classic kratom-and-kava beverage, along with five Palm Beach County retailers, according to [CBS 12 (WPEC)](https://cbs12.com/news/local/family-kevin-oliveira-palm-beach-county-man-lawsuit-kratom-drink-maker-local-retailers-after-32-year-olds-death-feel-free-classic-botanic-tonics-wrongful-death-wellness).

The lawsuit alleges that Oliveira began consuming Feel Free in 2023, believing it was a safe plant-based wellness product based on the company's marketing. According to the complaint, the District 19 Medical Examiner determined his cause of death on April 21, 2025, was "Toxic Effects of Mitragynine," the primary active alkaloid in kratom. The family's lawsuit alleges Oliveira experienced severe symptoms including vomiting, seizures, and psychosis before his death.

The complaint names legal claims including strict liability, failure to warn, design defect, negligence, breach of implied warranty, and negligent misrepresentation. The family is seeking damages under Florida's Wrongful Death Act.

The lawsuit comes days after the [federal government dismissed its own enforcement case against Botanic Tonics][botanic-tonics-case-dismissed-new-york-alabama-kratom], a development covered earlier this week. The dismissed federal case and the new private lawsuit represent different legal avenues targeting the same company and product.

## Broader context: Feel Free under scrutiny

A CBS Chicago investigation published June 1 found that Illinois poison control reports involving kratom [doubled in 2025](https://www.cbsnews.com/chicago/news/kratom-drink-feel-free-addiction-health-death/), with the state recording 145 reports, more than double the prior year's figure. The investigation reported that doctors treating kratom dependency are using buprenorphine, the same medication prescribed for heroin and fentanyl addiction. Dr. Elisabeth Poorman, quoted in the CBS investigation, said that some Feel Free users "need higher doses of buprenorphine, sometimes, even than my patients who are using fentanyl."

Botanic Tonics has said it supports increased kratom regulation and has cited clinical trial data in distinguishing its products from synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) preparations. The company did not respond to CBS Chicago's interview requests, according to the report.
