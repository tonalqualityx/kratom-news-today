Tennessee's legislature passed a full kratom ban after weeks of debate, sending the measure to the governor's desk. If signed, the ban would take effect July 1, according to [KATV](https://katv.com/news/nation-world/gas-station-heroin-tn-passes-full-kratom-ban-after-weeks-long-debate-over-effects). The action makes Tennessee one of the most restrictive states in the country on kratom, moving beyond the targeted 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) approach that other states have favored.

The Tennessee vote came the same day that attorneys general in Missouri and Alabama announced separate enforcement actions against kratom businesses, adding to an accelerating pattern of state-level regulatory pressure on the industry.

## Tennessee: Full Ban Clears Legislature

Tennessee lawmakers approved a measure that would prohibit the sale of kratom statewide, [KATV reported](https://katv.com/news/nation-world/gas-station-heroin-tn-passes-full-kratom-ban-after-weeks-long-debate-over-effects). The bill passed after weeks of debate over the substance's effects and its classification. The ban now goes to the governor for signature and, if signed, would take effect July 1.

The Tennessee approach is notably broader than recent action in other states. Where the [FDA's scheduling recommendation][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] and several state laws have focused specifically on concentrated 7-OH products, Tennessee's measure covers kratom itself. The distinction matters for the industry: a 7-OH-focused restriction leaves natural kratom leaf products on shelves, while a full ban removes the entire category.

Tennessee joins a small group of states that have enacted or are pursuing outright kratom prohibitions, in contrast to the consumer-protection-act model that the American Kratom Association (AKA) has advocated in other legislatures. The [Idaho Falls city council unanimously banned kratom sales][idaho-falls-kratom-ban-botanic-tonics-wrongful-death] last week after the county coroner linked the substance to six deaths in 18 months.

## Missouri AG Opens Statewide Investigation

Missouri Attorney General Catherine Hanaway announced a statewide investigation into kratom manufacturers and distributors, the [Missouri AG's office said](https://ago.mo.gov/attorney-general-hanaway-launches-investigation-into-major-kratom-manufacturers-and-retailers-for-potentially-dangerous-and-illegal-practices/). Hanaway's office issued six civil investigative demands (CIDs) to companies it described as manufacturing, distributing, or selling kratom products that may violate state law.

The AG's office said the investigation targets what it called "unapproved, unsafe, and deceptively marketed opioid-like drugs," according to the announcement. The office said it is focused on major distributors and smoke shops selling kratom products within the state.

## Alabama AG Issues Cease-and-Desist

Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall's office issued a statewide cease-and-desist order against businesses selling kratom products containing mitragynine or 7-hydroxymitragynine, the [Alabama AG's office announced](https://www.alabamaag.gov/alabama-attorney-general-issues-statewide-cease-and-desist-order-targeting-illegal-kratom-products/). Marshall's office said lab testing confirmed those substances in products on store shelves and that the office is coordinating enforcement with local prosecutors and law enforcement.

Under Alabama's controlled-substances law, kratom products containing mitragynine or hydroxymitragynine are illegal to possess, sell, or offer for sale in the state regardless of labeling, according to the AG's office.

## AKA Responds With Advisory

The AKA issued a formal policy advisory urging attorneys general in states with Kratom Consumer Protection Acts to pursue enforcement against concentrated 7-OH products specifically, [The Media Mix reported](https://www.themediamix.co/p/american-kratom-association-issues). The advisory noted the FDA's [recommendation to schedule 7-OH][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] and emphasized the distinction between concentrated 7-OH products and natural kratom leaf.

The AKA's position, consistent with its recent legislative advocacy, frames the regulatory question as one of targeted enforcement against concentrated products rather than broad prohibition. Missouri's investigation and Alabama's cease-and-desist order, which cover kratom products broadly rather than 7-OH specifically, represent the enforcement approach the AKA has argued against.

## Broader Context

The three state-level actions arrive amid an active federal regulatory push. The FDA [recommended scheduling concentrated 7-OH products][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] earlier this month, issued warning letters to seven companies, and sent advisories to physicians and consumers. A [CDC report][cdc-kratom-poison-center-calls-surge] published in late May found that kratom-related poison center calls rose approximately 1,200 percent from 2015 to 2025, reaching 3,434 reports.

The pattern of state action continues to split along two tracks: states pursuing targeted restrictions on 7-OH products (following the FDA's approach) and states pursuing broader kratom prohibitions or enforcement actions covering all kratom alkaloids. The Tennessee ban awaits the governor's signature, and both the Missouri and Alabama actions are in early stages.
