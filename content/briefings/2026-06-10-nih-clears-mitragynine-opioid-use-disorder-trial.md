The National Institutes of Health announced that its Investigational New Drug (IND) application for mitragynine, the primary alkaloid in kratom, took effect with the Food and Drug Administration on June 1, [the agency said in a news release](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-research-clears-way-study-experimental-treatment-opioid-use-disorder). The clearance opens the door to an NIH-led Phase 1 clinical trial evaluating purified mitragynine as an experimental treatment for opioid use disorder, the first human study of purified mitragynine.

The announcement places one arm of the federal government in the position of studying a kratom alkaloid as a possible addiction treatment at the same time another arm, the FDA, is pressing to schedule concentrated [7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] and maintains that kratom itself is not lawfully marketed in the United States.

## The Trial: 32 Volunteers, Single Ascending Doses

NIH and University of Florida researchers will test a purified formulation of mitragynine in 32 healthy volunteers with a recent history of opioid use, according to the NIH release. The study uses a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, single-ascending-dose design. Participants will be observed in a clinic for three nights through Day 4, then return for a follow-up visit on Day 7.

The trial's primary focus is safety and tolerability, [Pain News Network reported](https://www.painnewsnetwork.org/stories/2026/6/4/feds-to-study-kratom-as-treatment-for-opioid-use-disorder). Preclinical studies led by scientists at the University of Florida and at NIH's National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences (NCATS) and National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) found that mitragynine administration across several doses did not raise significant safety concerns in animal models, according to the NIH release.

"We've seen the potential of mitragynine in the lab, and now we're finally able to examine its potential in people," NCATS Director Joni Rutter said in the announcement. NIDA Director Nora Volkow called the study a major step toward expanding treatment options for opioid use disorder.

## Years of Groundwork at the University of Florida

The IND caps a research program that has been building for the better part of a decade. The University of Florida has held multiple NIDA grants for kratom research, including a five-year, $3.4 million award that concluded in 2024, and UF researcher Christopher McCurdy [told WUSF in 2024](https://www.wusf.org/health-news-florida/2024-05-09/kratom-research-florida-university-leader-questions-remain) that the program's stated goal was an FDA investigational new drug application for purified mitragynine in capsule form. The June 1 announcement makes that goal concrete.

UF's College of Pharmacy hosted the Fifth Scientific Kratom Symposium in Orlando in February, [drawing about 100 researchers for nearly 50 presentations](https://pharmacy.ufl.edu/2026/02/23/symposium-spotlights-emerging-science-involving-kratom/) on kratom science.

## A Study That Cuts Against the Regulatory Current

The trial clearance lands in a regulatory environment that has been moving in the opposite direction. The FDA [recommended earlier this month that the DEA schedule concentrated 7-OH products](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers), issued warning letters to seven companies, and has long maintained that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a drug, dietary supplement, or food additive. A bipartisan group of senators has [urged the FDA to pursue scheduling for the whole kratom leaf][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down], and a [CDC analysis published in late May][cdc-kratom-poison-center-calls-surge] reported that kratom-related poison center calls rose approximately 1,200 percent from 2015 to 2025.

The American Kratom Association cast the trial as a counterweight to that pressure. The AKA's Mac Haddow told Pain News Network that the decision "sends exactly the opposite message" from prior federal opposition to kratom, framing the trial as recognition that the plant's primary alkaloid merits clinical study.

The distinction at the center of the federal posture remains the one regulators have drawn repeatedly this year: mitragynine and natural leaf kratom on one side, concentrated and synthetic 7-OH preparations on the other. The NIH trial studies the former. The FDA's scheduling push targets the latter.

## What Comes Next

Phase 1 results will determine whether mitragynine advances to larger efficacy trials, a process that typically spans years. The trial does not change kratom's regulatory status: the FDA's position that kratom is not lawfully marketed as a drug, dietary supplement, or food additive remains in force while the study proceeds.
