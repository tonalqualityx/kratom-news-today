The New York State Legislature passed legislation prohibiting the sale of synthetic kratom products containing 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), with both chambers approving the measure unanimously in the closing days of the session, [Spectrum News reported](https://spectrumlocalnews.com/nys/central-ny/politics/2026/06/08/kratom-7oh-gas-station-heroin). The bill now heads to Governor Kathy Hochul, who signed two narrower kratom laws earlier this month.

The measure, advanced in the Assembly as [A10969](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/A10969) under the name Synthetic Kratom Kills Act, targets lab-produced 7-OH preparations rather than natural leaf kratom. A Senate version of the act, [introduced by Senator Scarcella-Spanton in May][new-york-synthetic-kratom-kills-act-documentary-premieres], drew the same distinction between synthetic products and the plant itself.

## A Unanimous, Bipartisan Vote

Assemblymember Phil Steck, a Democrat who chairs the Assembly's Committee on Alcoholism and Drug Abuse, sponsored the bill with Republican cosponsors Mary Beth Walsh and Jake Blumencranz, [according to the Assembly's announcement](https://assembly.state.ny.us/mem/Phil-Steck/story/118530). The announcement said the legislation builds on labeling legislation previously authored by Steck and Walsh and would place New York alongside seven other states that have enacted similar restrictions on 7-OH products.

"We want to save as many people from this evil substance," Steck told Spectrum News. Blumencranz said doctors have described 7-OH as "an entry point into the fourth wave of the opioid crisis," according to the same report.

Chinazo Cunningham, commissioner of the state Office of Addiction Services and Supports, drew the line between the concentrated products and the plant in comments to Spectrum News: "It's way more intense than kratom itself."

The Assembly announcement said the bill was motivated in part by the November 2024 death of Nick Scribner, a Ballston Spa resident. The Assembly release described 7-OH products as approximately 13 times more potent than morphine and noted they are often marketed under the shorthand "gas station heroin." The products are typically sold in gas stations, convenience stores, and smoke shops, Spectrum News reported.

## New York's Third Kratom Action This Month

The passage caps an active stretch for kratom policy in Albany. Hochul [signed two laws on June 3][botanic-tonics-case-dismissed-new-york-alabama-kratom] restricting kratom sales to adults 21 and older and requiring warning labels and ingredient disclosure on kratom products, framing the measures as a first step toward regulating the category. The 7-OH ban would go further for synthetic products, removing them from sale entirely while leaving the regulated natural leaf market in place.

The structure mirrors the approach taken by federal regulators. The FDA [recommended on June 1 that the DEA schedule concentrated 7-OH products](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers) under the Controlled Substances Act and issued warning letters to seven companies distributing 7-OH tablets, gummies, drink mixes, and shots. The agency said that action targets 7-OH products and [does not extend to natural kratom leaf][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban]. Spectrum News reported that the FDA has designated 7-OH an emerging public health threat.

## What Comes Next

The bill awaits Hochul's signature. The governor has not publicly indicated a timeline for acting on it, though her office described the June 3 age-restriction law as "a first logical step to regulate kratom in New York" [in its signing announcement](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-signs-two-pieces-landmark-legislation-protect-new-yorkers-against-harmful). If signed, New York would join the group of states that have separated synthetic 7-OH products from natural leaf kratom in statute, an approach advocacy organizations including the American Kratom Association and the Global Kratom Coalition have endorsed while opposing broader bans.
