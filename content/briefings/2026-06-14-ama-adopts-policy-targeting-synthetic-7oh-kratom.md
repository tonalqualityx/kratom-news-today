The American Medical Association adopted a policy at its 2026 annual meeting in Chicago that calls for legislation banning synthesized and derivative kratom products and urges the FDA and state legislatures to restrict concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products sold in forms that appeal to children. The policy amends the association's existing position on kratom, H-95.903, "Regulate Kratom and Ban Over-The-Counter Sales." The text of the amended policy appears in the report of the meeting's [Reference Committee B](https://www.ama-assn.org/system/files/a26-refcomm-b-report.pdf), which recommended its adoption.

## What the Policy Says

The amended policy directs the AMA to pursue legislation banning the marketing, distribution, promotion, and sale of synthesized, purified, or derivative kratom products, a category the policy text identifies as including unregulated mitragynine, 7-OH, and the derivative MGM-15. It also urges the FDA and state legislatures to classify 7-OH kratom products as adulterated or misbranded when sold in child-appealing forms, and to prohibit their availability in physical retail stores and online platforms accessible to minors, per the reference committee report.

The policy further recommends that the safety and efficacy of kratom and its derivatives be established through research and clinical trials and evaluated by regulators, including for potential oversight under the Controlled Substances Act, before the products are marketed or prescribed. Two provisions address consumers directly: the policy recommends that people currently using kratom for pain management or other conditions have access to medical care for those conditions and for withdrawal symptoms, and that people who use kratom only for personal use not face criminal consequences. The policy also calls for physician-led public education campaigns warning parents and young people about 7-OH risks, which it describes as including adverse skin effects, and for research into pediatric exposures.

## How It Came Together

The policy originated as Resolution 201, "Prohibit and Regulate 7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) Kratom Products to Protect Public Health and Youth Safety," submitted by the Oklahoma, Mississippi, and South Carolina delegations. Reference Committee B recommended that an alternate version of Resolution 201 be adopted in lieu of both that measure and Resolution 233, "Banning Synthesized, Purified or Derivative Products from Kratom," combining the two into a single policy, the committee report shows.

## Industry Response

The American Kratom Association, the industry's largest advocacy group, [responded on June 10](https://www.accessnewswire.com/newsroom/en/healthcare-and-pharmaceutical/american-kratom-association-calls-on-ama-to-properly-identify-the-rea-1175916), welcoming the AMA's attention to concentrated 7-OH while arguing that the policy did not go far enough in separating those products from natural leaf kratom. "The AMA correctly identifies the danger posed by concentrated 7-OH products, but it stops short of making the most important distinction," said Mac Haddow, the association's senior fellow on public policy. The group's position is that natural kratom leaf contains mitragynine as its dominant alkaloid with 7-OH present only in trace amounts, while the products drawing regulatory concern reverse that ratio through chemical conversion. The association said the AMA and policymakers should direct enforcement toward the synthesized versions rather than traditional leaf.

## The Broader 7-OH Picture

The AMA policy lands alongside parallel federal and state action on the same compound. The FDA [recommended on June 1 that the DEA schedule concentrated 7-OH products][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] while distinguishing them from natural leaf, and the American Kratom Association has separately [urged state attorneys general to enforce existing law against 7-OH products][aka-urges-ag-enforcement-7oh-florida-ban-expiry]. New York's legislature [passed its own synthetic-kratom ban][new-york-legislature-passes-synthetic-kratom-7oh-ban] in June. Percentage caps on 7-OH are already law in some states: Colorado's 2025 Kratom Regulation statute caps 7-OH at 2 percent of total alkaloids and bans synthetic and semi-synthetic kratom, and Mississippi's 2025 Kratom Consumer Protection Act sets a 1 percent ceiling, as detailed in KNT's [State of Kratom Regulation report](/reports/state-of-kratom-regulation-2026/).

The AMA policy is a set of advocacy positions, not law. It commits the association to lobby the FDA and state legislatures and to mount physician education campaigns, rather than itself changing the legal status of any product.
