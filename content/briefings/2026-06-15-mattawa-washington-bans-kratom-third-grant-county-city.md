The Mattawa City Council voted at its June 4 meeting to ban the sale, distribution, and advertising of all kratom products within city limits, the Columbia Basin Herald [reported](https://columbiabasinherald.com/news/2026/jun/12/mattawa-becomes-third-in-grant-co-to-ban-kratom/) on June 12. Mattawa is the third municipality in Grant County to prohibit kratom, following Royal City and Warden.

Ordinance 26-707 creates a new chapter in the Mattawa Municipal Code that defines "kratom product" broadly to include food, supplements, beverages, powders, capsules, and extracts, whether natural or synthetic, according to the Columbia Basin Herald. Violations are classified as civil infractions with escalating penalties for repeat offenses, and retailers that do not comply face potential business license revocation. The ordinance takes effect 60 days after publication, placing the effective date in early August 2026.

The council's findings cited the lack of FDA approval for kratom or its derivatives for medical use, the FDA's recommendation that 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) be scheduled under the federal Controlled Substances Act, and concerns about youth access and toxicity risks when kratom is combined with other substances, the Columbia Basin Herald reported.

Mattawa's action extends a pattern of [municipal-level kratom bans across Washington state][washington-local-governments-fill-kratom-regulatory-vacuum] that accelerated after the state legislature failed to pass a statewide regulatory framework. Spokane, Spokane Valley, Cle Elum, and Othello have all enacted their own bans in 2026, and a Municipal Research and Services Center analysis published in May warned that additional municipalities were considering similar action.

The city-level bans share a common template: broad product definitions covering both natural and synthetic kratom, penalties targeting retailers, and legislative findings that reference FDA and DEA risk characterizations. Grant County's three bans concentrate the restrictions in a rural corridor in central Washington where convenience retail and vape shops had been primary kratom sales channels.

Mattawa's ordinance arrives as local regulatory action on kratom continues to spread beyond Washington. A Plymouth, Massachusetts Board of Health [regulation](https://plymouth-ma.gov/DocumentCenter/View/10780/Kratom-Regulations-Effective-7-1-2026-PDF) posted this month, also effective July 1, 2026, characterizes kratom use as capable of causing psychotic symptoms and physiological dependence. The Plymouth regulation, like the Grant County bans, relies on federal agency risk assessments as its stated justification.

At the state level, [Tennessee's full kratom ban takes effect July 1][tennessee-kratom-ban-missouri-alabama-ag-enforcement], classifying the primary kratom alkaloids as controlled substances and imposing criminal penalties for sale, possession, and distribution. The convergence of municipal bans in Washington, local health board action in Massachusetts, and a statewide prohibition in Tennessee reflects a regulatory landscape where multiple levels of government are acting independently rather than waiting for federal scheduling.
