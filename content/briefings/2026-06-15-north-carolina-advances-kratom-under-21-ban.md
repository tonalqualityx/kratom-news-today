A North Carolina House committee unanimously advanced SB 59 this week after adopting an amendment that specifically adds kratom products to the bill's under-21 sales ban, Marijuana Moment [reported](https://www.marijuanamoment.net/north-carolina-lawmakers-advance-bill-to-set-a-minimum-age-limit-for-hemp-and-kratom-products/) on June 13. The bill, which originally targeted hemp-derived consumable products, now prohibits the sale and possession of kratom by anyone under 21.

The committee approved both the kratom amendment and the underlying bill without debate, according to Marijuana Moment. Retailers would be required to verify identification if they have "reasonable grounds" to believe a buyer is under 21. Violations would carry a Class 2 misdemeanor with escalating fines: $500 for a first offense, $1,000 for a second, and $1,500 for subsequent offenses.

Lawmakers indicated the bill now moves to the House Rules Committee, and additional amendments are expected on the House floor, Marijuana Moment reported. A floor vote could be scheduled as soon as next week.

The kratom amendment groups the substance alongside other hemp-derived psychoactive products in North Carolina's regulatory approach, treating it as a consumer product requiring age restriction rather than pursuing an outright ban. The approach contrasts with Tennessee, where a [full kratom prohibition takes effect July 1][tennessee-kratom-ban-missouri-alabama-ag-enforcement], and with municipal bans in Washington state that prohibit all kratom sales regardless of the buyer's age.

North Carolina's kratom regulatory landscape has been developing on a separate track as well. A pending state bill would require kratom manufacturers and distributors to obtain [state licenses by July 1, 2026][california-kratom-sales-bill-north-carolina-licensing], adding a licensing framework on top of the age restriction now moving through the legislature. Whether SB 59's kratom provisions interact with the licensing bill's requirements remains unclear from the committee record.

The bill's progress adds North Carolina to a growing list of states opting for age-restriction models rather than outright bans. Several states, including Utah, Nevada, and New York, have enacted 21-and-older sales restrictions in 2026, though enforcement mechanisms and penalty structures vary.
