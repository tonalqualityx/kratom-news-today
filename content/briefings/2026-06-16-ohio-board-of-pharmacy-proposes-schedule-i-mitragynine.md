The Ohio Board of Pharmacy has moved from deliberation to formal rulemaking on natural kratom. The Board [published a proposed rule](https://www.pharmacy.ohio.gov) to classify mitragynine compounds as Schedule I controlled substances under Ohio law, according to the Board's website. A public hearing is scheduled for June 23, 2026, and written comments are due by June 30.

The proposal marks a significant escalation from the Board's earlier posture. When Ohio's [permanent synthetic kratom ban took effect in May][ohio-eyes-natural-kratom-ban-poison-center-calls-surge], the Board was described as "considering" whether to extend restrictions to natural kratom. That consideration has now produced a formal rule package submitted through Ohio's Common Sense Initiative (CSI) regulatory review process, with supporting documents including a consumer and retailer notice on the sale and possession of kratom in the state.

## What the proposed rule would do

The proposed rule, titled "Classification of Mitragynine Compounds as Schedule I Controlled Substances," would place mitragynine and related alkaloids found in kratom leaf (Mitragyna speciosa) under Ohio's Schedule I. If finalized, the rule would effectively ban possession and sale of all kratom products in the state, not just the synthetic derivatives already prohibited under the [permanent ban that took effect May 14][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect].

Ohio's existing framework distinguishes between synthetic kratom compounds (banned) and natural kratom leaf (legal). The proposed rule would eliminate that distinction. A separate document posted by the Board, titled "Consumer and Retailer Notice," addresses the sale and possession of kratom in Ohio, signaling that the Board is preparing both the regulatory mechanism and the public communication around potential enforcement.

## Legislative and regulatory tension

The timing creates a direct tension with Ohio's legislative track. Representatives Mike Odioso and Brian Lorenz [introduced HB 587, the Kratom Consumer Protection Act][missouri-ag-expands-kratom-probe-ohio-consumer-protection-act], on May 29. That bill would regulate natural kratom through licensing, quality standards, and consumer protections rather than ban it outright. The Board's proposed rule would render that regulatory framework moot by classifying the underlying compounds as Schedule I before HB 587 could move through committee.

The collision between a pharmacy board's scheduling authority and a legislative consumer-protection approach is not unique to Ohio, but the simultaneity is unusual. The Board's rulemaking can proceed through the administrative process without requiring legislative approval, though the CSI review process does allow for public input and a business-impact analysis.

## Comment period and what comes next

The public hearing on June 23 and the June 30 comment deadline will be the primary opportunities for stakeholders to weigh in before the Board makes a final determination. Industry advocates, consumer groups, and legislators backing HB 587 will all have standing to submit comments through the CSI process.

If the Board finalizes the rule, Ohio would join a small number of states that classify natural kratom alongside synthetic derivatives. Tennessee's full kratom ban, signed in May, takes effect July 1. Kansas signed a ban on 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) substances into law in May as well. But most states that have acted on kratom have targeted synthetic and concentrated 7-OH products specifically, leaving natural kratom leaf legal.

The Board's proposed rule is available on the [Ohio Board of Pharmacy website](https://www.pharmacy.ohio.gov), along with the consumer and retailer notice and a CSI comments document.
