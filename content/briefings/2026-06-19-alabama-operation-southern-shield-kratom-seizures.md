Alabama law enforcement agencies seized nearly 64,000 bottles of kratom-containing products in a coordinated enforcement operation this week, according to a [report from WBRC](https://www.yahoo.com/news/us/articles/authorities-target-illegal-sale-products-234600968.html) published June 18.

The operation, dubbed Operation Southern Shield, involved the Alabama Law Enforcement Agency (ALEA), the Anniston Police Department, the 7th Judicial Major Crimes Task Force, the Alabama Beverage Control Board, and the Calhoun County District Attorney's office, the report stated. As of press time, authorities had executed 83 search warrants across multiple Alabama cities and seized a total of 63,995 bottles.

The largest single seizure occurred at a location in the 2000 block of Coleman Road in Anniston, where authorities recovered 2,289 bottles of Xuno, a product the report identified as containing kratom (mitragynine or hydroxymitragynine). A second location in the 3000 block of US Highway 78 in Anniston yielded 88 bottles of Mind Lift, 24 bottles of Groovy Brew, 34 bottles of Good Day, one bottle of Wava Kava, and 45 bottles of Xuno.

Anniston Police Chief Clint Parris said enforcement would continue. "The time for warnings is over. We will enforce the law," Parris stated, according to the report.

## Legal Context

Alabama classified kratom as a Schedule I controlled substance in 2016, making the sale and possession of products containing its alkaloids illegal under state law. Five other states maintain full kratom bans: Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Vermont, and Wisconsin.

The enforcement action follows Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall's [statewide cease-and-desist order][tennessee-kratom-ban-missouri-alabama-ag-enforcement] issued earlier this month targeting businesses selling kratom products. That order, issued in early June, put retailers on notice that continued kratom sales would face legal consequences.

The volume of the seizures (nearly 64,000 bottles across 83 locations) underscores the scope of the enforcement effort, which followed the AG's warning that continued kratom sales would face legal consequences.

Calhoun County District Attorney Lynn Hammond's office participated in the operation, according to the report. The Alabama Beverage Control Board was also involved, though the report did not specify whether license actions against retailers would follow.
