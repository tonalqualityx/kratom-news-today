Allegheny County, Pennsylvania, ranks second in the Northeast for kratom-related emergency calls, according to a [Pittsburgh Post-Gazette investigation](https://www.post-gazette.com/news/health/2026/06/18/kratom-allegheny-county-pa/stories/202606210023) published June 18. The reporting, which draws on poison center data, hospital interviews, and patient accounts, arrives as state lawmakers prepare consumer protection legislation targeting the unregulated product category.

Pennsylvania poison centers received 167 kratom-related exposure reports in 2023 and 2024, the Post-Gazette reported. Of those, 25 patients received naloxone for kratom overdose, and 14 required mechanical intubation. Only Erie County recorded more kratom-related emergency calls in the region.

Joshua Shulman, medical director of the Pittsburgh Poison Center and a UPMC emergency physician, told the Post-Gazette that emergency departments are seeing a rising caseload. "We are seeing more than we used to, and we're going to see more," Shulman said. "I see it in the hospital for bad withdrawal."

Brent Rau, director of emergency medicine at Allegheny General Hospital, described kratom's retail availability as a contributing factor. "It's available like a bag of chips is available," Rau told the paper.

The figures align with a broader national trend identified in a [CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report][cdc-kratom-poison-center-calls-surge] published earlier this month, which found that U.S. poison centers received 3,434 kratom-related exposure reports in 2025, a roughly 1,200 percent increase over a decade.

## State Legislation Takes Shape

A cohort of Democratic House members announced in May that they would introduce the Kratom Standards, Labeling and Consumer Protection Act, according to the Post-Gazette. The bill, cosponsored by House Health Committee Chair Dan Frankel (D-Allegheny), would limit all kratom sales to adults 21 and older, ban synthetic kratom products such as concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), and strengthen labeling and advertising rules.

The Pennsylvania Society of Addiction Medicine (PSAM) wrote to state lawmakers in December 2025 supporting legislation banning kratom sales to anyone under 21, the paper reported. PSAM President James Latronica, a Pittsburgh-based addiction medicine physician, has been among those pushing for the regulatory framework.

A separate bill, Senate Bill 899, was temporarily postponed by Senate Majority Leader Joe Pittman (R-Armstrong/Indiana) in March 2026, the Post-Gazette noted.

Pittsburgh City Council voted on June 9 to tighten vape-shop regulations and ban new vape shops in the city, in part to address unregulated substances including kratom being sold in those outlets. The council vote followed public testimony from consumers who described dependence on kratom products purchased at local retailers.

## Industry Perspective

Mac Haddow of the American Kratom Association told the Post-Gazette that consumers need to scrutinize product labels. "The consumer has to look at what they're purchasing and the label," Haddow said. The AKA has supported age restrictions and FDA efforts to ban concentrated 7-OH products while defending access to natural leaf kratom.

The Post-Gazette investigation also noted that the FDA's July 2025 crackdown on 7-OH products and the broader [state-level enforcement pattern][tennessee-kratom-ban-missouri-alabama-ag-enforcement] have intensified attention on how kratom products reach consumers. Vita McCabe, director of addiction treatment services at the University of Michigan, described the regulatory gap to the paper. "From my perspective, this is a wolf in sheep's clothing," McCabe said.

Chris Holstege, director of UVA's Blue Ridge Poison Center, told the Post-Gazette that public awareness has not kept pace with the product's availability. "I do worry significantly that the public doesn't fully understand the risk with these products," Holstege said.

The Post-Gazette reported that an estimated five million Americans have used kratom in their lifetime, including roughly 100,000 children, citing federal survey data.
