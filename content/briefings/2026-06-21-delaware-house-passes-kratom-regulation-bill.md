The Delaware House of Representatives passed HB 332 on June 16, sending a kratom consumer protection bill to the state Senate. The measure, sponsored by Rep. Melanie Ross Levin (D-North Wilmington), would ban synthetic kratom products, restrict sales to buyers 21 and older, and require manufacturers to label alkaloid content accurately. The bill now faces a Senate committee that is also weighing SB 262, a competing proposal from Sen. Kyra Hoffner (D-Leipsic) that would ban kratom sales outright.

The two bills represent opposite approaches to a substance that Delaware currently does not specifically regulate. HB 332 takes the consumer protection route: it would prohibit kratom products containing contaminants, synthetically derived compounds, or 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) concentrations above naturally occurring levels. Packaging designed to appeal to minors, including images of superheroes, celebrities, or cartoon characters, would be banned. Violations would be classified as a Class B misdemeanor under the bill's text.

Ross Levin [told WHYY](https://whyy.org/articles/delaware-legislation-kratom-regulation/) her bill accounts for consumers who report using kratom for harm-reduction purposes, including those managing pain or recovering from opioid dependence. The distinction matters: during earlier committee testimony, Delaware resident Misty Brown described using kratom to manage cravings after years of pain medication and street drug use, telling lawmakers the substance helped her maintain six and a half years of sobriety, [according to WHYY](https://whyy.org/articles/delaware-legislation-kratom-regulation/).

The competing bill, SB 262, takes a different position. Sen. Hoffner has [said she does not believe any kratom product is safe](https://whyy.org/articles/delaware-legislation-kratom-regulation/) and has cited the binding affinity of mitragynine and 7-OH to opioid receptors as grounds for prohibition. Her bill would ban the manufacture, marketing, distribution, and sale of all kratom products in Delaware. Hoffner amended the bill to remove criminal penalties for simple possession after earlier drafts drew criticism for proposing Class C felony charges.

The Senate Health and Social Services Committee is expected to consider both measures. [When SB 262 was introduced](https://legis.delaware.gov/BillDetail/143097) in March, it drew split testimony from public health officials and kratom users, according to reporting from [Citizen Portal](https://citizenportal.ai/articles/7768500/delaware/legislative/2026-legislature-de/senators-hear-split-testimony-on-proposed-kratom-ban-sponsor-cites-health-risks-users-describe-benefits).

Delaware's legislative activity arrives amid a national pattern of state-level kratom regulation that has intensified in 2026. [Earlier this year, Delaware was weighing SB 262 alongside kratom bills in Arizona and elsewhere][arizona-kratom-bill-delaware-weighs-ban]. The American Kratom Association (AKA), the industry's largest advocacy group, has publicly backed the regulatory approach over outright bans. AKA spokesperson Mac Haddow told the [Pittsburgh Post-Gazette](https://www.post-gazette.com/news/health/2026/06/18/kratom-allegheny-county-pa/stories/202606210023) on June 18 that the organization supports age restrictions and the FDA's push to ban products containing synthetic 7-OH. Haddow attributed recent spikes in overdoses and poison center calls to mislabeled products with undisclosed synthetic 7-OH content, [not to properly labeled natural kratom powder][pennsylvania-kratom-ems-hotspot-legislation].

HB 332's provisions parallel elements of the model Kratom Consumer Protection Act that the AKA has promoted across multiple states, including age limits, product purity standards, and bans on synthetically derived compounds. Whether the Senate committee advances the regulation bill, the ban, or some combination of the two remains to be seen. Both bills are before the same committee, and neither has been scheduled for a vote as of June 21.
