Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier signed an emergency rule on June 22 that places concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) and several related compounds into Schedule I under Florida's controlled substances law, effective immediately. [According to the Attorney General's office](https://www.myfloridalegal.com/newsrelease/attorney-general-james-uthmeier-signs-emergency-rule-immediately-scheduling-dangerous-7), the rule classifies the compounds as Schedule I substances, a category for drugs the state treats as having no accepted medical use and a high potential for abuse, and it exposes manufacturers, distributors, and sellers to felony penalties of up to 30 years in prison.

The rule names seven compounds: 7-hydroxymitragynine, mitragynine pseudoindoxyl, 7-acetoxymitragynine, 9-hydroxycorynantheidine, 10-hydroxycorynantheidine, MGM-15, and MGM-16. The Attorney General's office said products may not contain more than one milligram of these substances per gram or per milliliter, and that any product containing 7-OH must contain at least 100 times more mitragynine by mass. [Fox 13 News reported](https://www.fox13news.com/news/florida-expands-emergency-ban-gas-station-morphine) that the action expands an earlier Florida emergency ban to cover "copycat chemical formulas" that the office said were designed to work around the prior restrictions.

The rule is aimed at concentrated 7-OH products rather than kratom leaf itself. The Attorney General's office said 7-OH occurs naturally in kratom (Mitragyna speciosa) at trace levels of roughly 0.01 to 0.04 percent, and that the scheduling applies to concentrated formulas that exceed the stated thresholds. The distinction tracks the position [the American Kratom Association has pressed on state attorneys general][aka-urges-ag-enforcement-7oh-florida-ban-expiry], which separates natural-leaf kratom from concentrated or synthetic 7-OH.

Uthmeier framed the rule as an urgent public health measure. "Today I signed an emergency rule because these dangerous concentrated 7-OH products present an imminent hazard to the public safety of Floridians, especially our children and teens," he said in [a statement from his office](https://www.myfloridalegal.com/newsrelease/attorney-general-james-uthmeier-signs-emergency-rule-immediately-scheduling-dangerous-7). He said the products can be "13 times or more potent than morphine" and described them as sold "on shelves next to candy bars." [Florida Voice News reported](https://flvoicenews.com/florida-expands-chemical-ban-on-synthetic-kratom-warning-bad-actors-face-30-year-prison-terms/) that violations can carry prison terms of up to 30 years.

The emergency rule follows an earlier Florida order on concentrated 7-OH that, [as KNT reported on June 1, was set to expire June 30][aka-urges-ag-enforcement-7oh-florida-ban-expiry] absent action by the legislature or the Board of Pharmacy. It also arrives as other states move on 7-OH specifically: [Ohio's Board of Pharmacy proposed Schedule I classification for mitragynine compounds][ohio-board-of-pharmacy-proposes-schedule-i-mitragynine] earlier this month. The Florida rule takes effect immediately, and the Attorney General's office said it enables felony arrests, prosecutions, product seizures, and business shutdowns for products that fall within the scheduled categories.
