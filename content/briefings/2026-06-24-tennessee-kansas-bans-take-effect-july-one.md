Two state-level kratom bans are now one week from taking effect. Tennessee's full prohibition and Kansas's Schedule I classification of kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) both go live on July 1, according to coverage in [The Tennessean](https://www.tennessean.com/story/news/politics/2026/06/23/tennessee-kratom-ban-july-1/90648150007/) and [Planet Ave](https://www.planetavenus.online/post/possessing-buying-or-selling-kratom-and-the-related-compound-7oh-will-be-a-crime-in-kansas-beginni). Separately, New York's Suffolk County Legislature held a public hearing on June 23 on a proposed ban on synthetic kratom products, [News 12 Long Island reported](https://longisland.news12.com/2026/06/23/suffolk-county-legislature-to-hold-public-hearing-today-on-proposed-kratom-ban/1PPJH8O8IGdzyGYQB5rIHD).

## Tennessee: "Matthew Davenport's Law"

Tennessee's kratom ban, known as "Matthew Davenport's Law," prohibits the sale of kratom and at least one other plant-based substance in the state, [The Tennessean reported](https://www.tennessean.com/story/news/politics/2026/06/23/tennessee-kratom-ban-july-1/90648150007/). The law creates criminal penalties for selling or possessing the banned products. [As KNT reported when the legislation passed][tennessee-kratom-ban-missouri-alabama-ag-enforcement], the measure makes Tennessee one of the most restrictive states in the country on kratom.

The Tennessean reported that the law was driven by concerns about health risks and abuse, as well as incidents underlying the law's namesake. Retailers currently selling kratom in the state, including gas stations, vape shops, and specialty shops, face loss of legal market access and possible criminal exposure after July 1.

## Kansas: kratom and 7-OH both scheduled

Kansas will make possessing, buying, or selling kratom and the alkaloid 7-hydroxymitragynine a crime beginning July 1, [according to Planet Ave](https://www.planetavenus.online/post/possessing-buying-or-selling-kratom-and-the-related-compound-7oh-will-be-a-crime-in-kansas-beginni). The law classifies both kratom and 7-OH as Schedule I controlled substances, subject to the penalties applicable to that designation.

[Kansas Gov. Laura Kelly signed the 7-OH scheduling bill in May][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax], when a broader bill to schedule all kratom products died in committee. The current reporting describes the law as covering kratom products broadly alongside 7-OH, with criminal charges and fines for violations, suggesting the scope may extend beyond the 7-OH-only bill that was signed. The framing points to a prohibition model rather than a regulated-market approach.

## Suffolk County considers synthetic kratom ban

On Long Island, the Suffolk County Legislature held a public hearing on June 23 on a proposed ban that targets "synthetic kratom" products, [News 12 Long Island reported](https://longisland.news12.com/2026/06/23/suffolk-county-legislature-to-hold-public-hearing-today-on-proposed-kratom-ban/1PPJH8O8IGdzyGYQB5rIHD). The bill would ban synthetic kratom products typically found in gas stations and smoke shops while exempting drinks steeped from natural kratom leaf.

The distinction between synthetic or concentrated products and natural leaf preparations mirrors the approach taken in [Florida's expanded emergency rule signed June 22][florida-emergency-rule-7oh-schedule-one], which targets concentrated 7-OH formulas while leaving natural kratom leaf unscheduled.

## Industry groups back the natural-leaf distinction

Two kratom advocacy organizations issued public statements in support of that leaf-versus-concentrate framework after Florida's action. The American Kratom Association (AKA) applauded the Florida emergency rule, saying it targets "chemically manipulated 7-OH opioids" while protecting access to natural kratom leaf, [according to Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/healthcare/articles/american-kratom-association-applauds-florida-201900491.html). The Global Kratom Coalition (GKC) commended Attorney General James Uthmeier for extending and expanding the state's ban on concentrated synthetic 7-OH and its synthetic derivatives, [according to a press release distributed by AI Journal](https://aijourn.com/global-kratom-coalition-commends-florida-for-extending-and-expanding-its-ban-on-concentrated-synthetic-7-oh-and-its-synthetic-derivatives/).

Both groups framed the Florida approach as a model that separates natural kratom products from concentrated or synthetic compounds with more opioid-like pharmacological profiles. For manufacturers and retailers, however, the July 1 bans in Tennessee and Kansas draw no such distinction: those states are banning kratom products across the board, natural leaf included.
