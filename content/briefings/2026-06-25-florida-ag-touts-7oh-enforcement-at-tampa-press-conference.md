Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier held a press conference at Tampa General Hospital on June 23 to emphasize the state's enforcement posture on concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products, one day after [signing an expanded emergency rule][florida-emergency-rule-7oh-schedule-one] placing the compounds in Schedule I. [WUSF reported](https://www.wusf.org/health-news-florida/2026-06-23/florida-extends-crackdown-on-dangerous-kratom-related-products-known-as-7-oh) that Uthmeier cited Florida medical examiner data linking at least 587 overdose deaths to 7-OH and related kratom-derived compounds. Florida Poison Control received 95 calls involving 7-OH and its derivatives in the first four months of 2026, a pace Uthmeier said was on track to exceed prior years, according to the WUSF report.

The press conference followed an earlier Florida order that had limited 7-OH sales by outlawing products with more than 0.04 percent concentration, [according to WUSF](https://www.wusf.org/health-news-florida/2026-06-23/florida-extends-crackdown-on-dangerous-kratom-related-products-known-as-7-oh). The expanded emergency rule tightens that standard: products may contain no more than one milligram of 7-OH or covered compounds per gram of solid product or per milliliter of liquid, and any product containing 7-OH must also contain at least 100 times more mitragynine by mass, a ratio threshold designed to exclude concentrated formulations while allowing products whose alkaloid profile resembles natural kratom leaf.

[St. Pete Catalyst reported](https://stpetecatalyst.com/attorney-general-discusses-7-oh-ban-at-tampa-general-hospital/) that Uthmeier referenced pharmacological data, including CDER materials describing 7-hydroxymitragynine as approximately 13 times more potent than morphine in a guinea pig ileum assay. The 587-death figure and the potency comparison are drawn from Florida medical examiner records and federal research references, respectively. The death figure has not been independently verified by KNT and may include cases involving polydrug toxicity, a distinction that advocacy groups have pressed in other contexts.

## Industry Response

The American Kratom Association (AKA) endorsed the enforcement action. In a statement titled "Florida AG Takes Decisive Action on 7-OH," [the AKA](https://www.americankratom.org) described the targeted products as "chemically manipulated 7-OH opioids" and drew a sharp line between those products and natural leaf kratom (Mitragyna speciosa). The AKA said the Florida action represents a model for other states: enforce against concentrated or synthetic 7-OH formulations that pose opioid-like risks rather than restricting unadulterated kratom products. The organization emphasized that 7-hydroxymitragynine occurs naturally at trace levels in kratom leaf and that the pharmacological risk profile differs significantly when the alkaloid is concentrated or chemically manipulated.

The Global Kratom Coalition (GKC) also commended the rule, [according to a statement published on June 22](https://aijourn.com/global-kratom-coalition-commends-florida-for-extending-and-expanding-its-ban-on-concentrated-synthetic-7-oh-and-its-synthetic-derivatives/). Both organizations framed the enforcement as evidence that states can address concentrated 7-OH products without scheduling kratom leaf itself, a position they have advanced in response to federal scheduling proposals.

## Policy Criticism

A June 23 commentary in [Florida Politics](https://floridapolitics.com/archives/803671-floridas-new-7-oh-ban-still-misses-the-bigger-kratom-problem/) argued that the emergency rule "misses the bigger kratom problem." The piece called for "comprehensive regulation, not a ban that protects one side," contending that a narrow alkaloid-level ban leaves broader product safety gaps unaddressed. The commentary did not identify its author's affiliations in the available text, though the argument aligns with positions frequently advanced by industry stakeholders who favor KCPA-style (Kratom Consumer Protection Act) regulatory frameworks over compound-specific scheduling.

The divergence between the AKA's endorsement and the Florida Politics critique illustrates a split within industry-aligned commentary. Both sides favor regulation over prohibition of natural kratom, but they disagree on whether targeting specific compounds is sufficient or whether broader product standards, including manufacturing oversight, labeling requirements, and age restrictions, should accompany any 7-OH enforcement.
