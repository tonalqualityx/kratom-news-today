Kootenai County Coroner Dr. Duke Johnson told county commissioners on June 25 that partial kratom regulations have failed to reduce harm and called for a total ban on kratom sales in the northern Idaho county. [Spokane Public Radio reported](https://www.spokanepublicradio.org/regional-news/2026-06-25/spr-news-today-coroner-pushes-kootenai-county-to-ban-kratom-sales-entirely-citing-rising-deaths) that Johnson cited 11 kratom-associated deaths in the county since 2021, with the annual count rising from three in 2021 to four in 2025.

Johnson told commissioners that concentration limits and age restrictions, the regulatory tools several other states have adopted, have not produced measurable reductions in hospitalizations, overdoses, or deaths. "In the states that had regulations to try to control kratom, there was no difference in hospitalizations, overdoses, or deaths compared to those states who had no regulations whatsoever," Johnson said, according to SPR. "There was a substantial reduction when it was banned."

The county had previously explored age restrictions and chemical concentration limits as alternatives to a full prohibition. Commissioners indicated that all cities within Kootenai County would need to support a ban before the county could implement one, and said they planned to circulate Johnson's findings to gather feedback and develop a consistent countywide policy, SPR reported.

Johnson's call for a ban comes as [Tennessee and Kansas prepare to enforce statewide kratom bans starting July 1][tennessee-kansas-bans-take-effect-july-one], and as several other jurisdictions weigh their own restrictions.

## Alabama Seizure Operation

Separately, Alabama law enforcement completed a week-long operation that resulted in the seizure of more than 64,000 bottles of kratom products from convenience stores across eight counties, [according to an AOL report](https://www.aol.com/articles/alabama-seizes-thousands-illegal-kratom-210040000.html). The operation, which involved the Alabama Law Enforcement Agency, the Attorney General's Office, and the State Bureau of Investigation, served 83 search warrants and identified two warehouses in Montgomery as distribution points.

Alabama has classified kratom as a controlled substance since 2016, making it one of the earliest states to ban the plant outright. The operation targeted retail outlets selling kratom products in violation of state law.

Shawn Loughridge, chief of the major crimes division at the State Bureau of Investigation, told reporters that kratom "can be highly addictive, and in small doses it acts as an aid for relaxation but in larger doses it is a stimulant." Katherine Robertson, chief counsel for the Attorney General's Office, said testing of seized products showed mislabeling of ingredients, which she described as making the products more dangerous, according to the report. Arrests were described as ongoing at the time of the announcement.

Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall issued a statewide cease-and-desist order earlier this month targeting retailers still selling kratom products. The seizure operation marks a significant escalation from that order, according to the report.
