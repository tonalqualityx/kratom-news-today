California's kratom enforcement campaign has produced more than $5 million in product seizures and 6,700 items pulled from retail shelves in six months, according to a San Francisco Chronicle investigation published this week. The report described what amounts to the most aggressive state-level crackdown on kratom products in the country, with enforcement now extending into civil litigation.

The California Department of Public Health (CDPH) declared kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) products illegal for consumption under state food safety laws, treating them as unsafe food additives, the [Chronicle reported](https://www.sfchronicle.com/sf/article/kratom-feel-free-drink-california-22302788.php). Under this classification, foods and drinks containing kratom are considered adulterated and illegal to manufacture or sell, even though kratom itself is not explicitly banned by California statute.

The Department of Alcoholic Beverage Control (ABC) conducted thousands of compliance visits to stores statewide and removed the 6,700-plus products, according to the Chronicle. CDPH reported the $5 million seizure figure covering the past six months through mid-2026.

## AG Bonta sues San Diego manufacturer

Enforcement escalated into civil litigation in May, when CDPH and Attorney General Rob Bonta filed a lawsuit against a San Diego-based kratom company that allegedly continued manufacturing and distributing kratom-derived products after the CDPH declaration, the Chronicle reported. The lawsuit represents a shift from store-level enforcement to targeting manufacturers directly.

The Chronicle also reported that kratom-derived beverages, including products marketed under the "Feel Free" brand, remain available at some California retailers despite the CDPH declaration. The gap between enforcement activity and continued retail availability raised questions about the consistency and reach of the state's approach.

## San Mateo County passes countywide ban

San Mateo County recently passed a countywide ban on the sale and distribution of kratom and 7-OH products, according to the Chronicle. Violations are treated as misdemeanors, punishable by fines of up to $1,000, up to six months in jail, and possible revocation of county-issued licenses including tobacco sales permits.

The county-level ban adds a layer of local enforcement on top of the statewide CDPH framework, a pattern also emerging in [other jurisdictions weighing local restrictions][florida-ag-touts-7oh-enforcement-at-tampa-press-conference].

## AKA calls enforcement "a knee-jerk reaction"

The American Kratom Association (AKA) pushed back on California's approach. Mac Haddow, the AKA's lead lobbyist, told the Chronicle that California's enforcement was "a knee-jerk reaction" to an FDA web page and that the AKA had advised California retailers the state's ban was not legally defensible. Haddow told retailers to consult counsel before deciding whether to keep products on shelves, according to the article.

Separately, [Pain News Network reported](https://www.painnewsnetwork.org/stories) that Haddow drew a distinction between product categories, saying "chemically manipulated 7-OH opioids are not traditional kratom products." The framing reflects the AKA's broader position that concentrated and synthetic 7-OH products should be distinguished from natural kratom leaf in regulatory frameworks.

## California hearing canceled amid federal action expectations

The Chronicle reported that a scheduled hearing on [Assembly Member Jasmeet Bains' kratom bill][california-kratom-sales-bill-north-carolina-licensing], which would impose age restrictions, child-resistant packaging requirements, and effectively ban concentrated 7-OH products, was canceled. Bains' office cited an expectation of imminent federal action on kratom and 7-OH as the reason for the cancellation.

California's enforcement approach differs from states like Florida, which has targeted concentrated 7-OH products specifically through emergency scheduling while leaving natural kratom leaf unregulated. California's framework treats all kratom-containing food and drink products as adulterated regardless of concentration, a broader classification that affects manufacturers of both leaf-based and concentrate-based products.
