A dietary supplement manufacturer has renewed its effort to block Utah's Kratom Regulation Act in federal court, filing a motion that argues the law's requirements make it impossible to manufacture kratom beverages in the state, [Law360 reported](https://www.law360.com/articles/2493348/kratom-interests-urge-court-again-to-halt-utah-law) on June 26.

The filing is the latest in an ongoing legal challenge to Utah's kratom framework, which [took effect in May][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate] after a federal judge denied a previous injunction request from the Global Kratom Coalition. The Act requires manufacturers to register products with the state and sets purity and labeling standards. The plaintiff in the renewed challenge argued that the law's alkaloid content, testing, or product-form requirements effectively exclude ready-to-drink kratom beverages from the market, though the specific provisions at issue were not detailed in available reporting.

The case carries broader significance. If the court agrees that a state's kratom regulations impose requirements that are unworkable for a specific product format, the ruling could set a precedent for how other states structure their own kratom consumer protection frameworks. Several states have adopted or are considering laws with concentration thresholds, testing mandates, and product-form restrictions that could face similar legal scrutiny from beverage-focused manufacturers.

## West Virginia opens comment period on product form rules

Separately, West Virginia's Secretary of State published a Notice of Public Comment Period on June 25 outlining proposed rules for kratom product approval and registration, according to the [state administrative filing](https://apps.sos.wv.gov/adlaw/csr/readfile.aspx?DocId=59070&Format=PDF). Under the proposed rules, effective as of June 12, the state would not approve or register any kratom product that is not in a permitted form.

The notice invites public comment on these restrictions, which appear to target specific product formats for exclusion from the regulated market. The exact product forms that would be prohibited or permitted were not fully detailed in the available filing summary.

West Virginia's approach reflects a growing regulatory pattern: states that have not banned kratom outright are instead channeling regulation through product form and registration requirements, restricting how kratom can be sold rather than whether it can be sold at all. This framework creates compliance obligations for manufacturers and distributors operating in or selling into the state, adding to the patchwork of state-level rules that the kratom industry has been adapting to throughout 2026.
