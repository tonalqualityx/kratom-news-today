Local officials in Kansas are preparing for the state's kratom ban to take effect July 1, with law enforcement and health departments describing both enforcement plans and steps to support current users. The Lawrence Journal-World reported on June 27 that [law enforcement and health officials in Lawrence are getting ready](https://www2.ljworld.com/news/public-safety/2026/jun/27/kansas-kratom-ban-begins-july-1-and-law-enforcement-and-health-officials-in-lawrence-are-preparing/) for the change, which classifies kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) as Schedule I controlled substances under House Bill 2365. The preparation follows the [enactment of the ban that KNT reported earlier this month][tennessee-kansas-bans-take-effect-july-one].

## Lawrence: enforcement and withdrawal readiness

According to the Lawrence Journal-World, House Bill 2365 makes kratom and products derived from it, including 7-OH, Schedule I under Kansas law, a designation the state applies to substances it has determined have high potential for abuse and no accepted medical use. The report said law enforcement is preparing to enforce the prohibition on possession, sale, and purchase beginning July 1.

The Journal-World also reported that health officials are preparing to treat people who experience withdrawal, anticipating that some current users may face painful symptoms if they stop abruptly after the ban takes effect. The framing in the report attributes that concern to the officials preparing for the change, who described the ban as part of the state's broader drug-control efforts. The article noted that kratom remains outside federal scheduling, and that Kansas acted independently when it passed the law.

## Johnson County publishes resources

In the Kansas City suburbs, Johnson County issued a public notice stating that kratom (mitragynine) and 7-OH will be illegal to possess, sell, or purchase beginning July 1, [according to the county government](https://www.jocogov.org/newsroom/johnson-county-offers-resources-ahead-july-1-kratom-and-7-oh-ban). The notice points residents to substance-use treatment and support services and explains how people who currently use the products can access help as the ban takes effect.

The county's messaging treats mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine as specific controlled alkaloids rather than referring to kratom generically, mirroring the language of the statute. State Affairs, framing the ban alongside other drug laws taking effect at the same time, reported that [Kansans will no longer be able to purchase or consume kratom and 7-OH products legally](https://pro.stateaffairs.com/ks/news/kansas-kratom-ban-nears-as-other-drug-laws-take-effect) once the full ban takes effect.

## Business and market effect

For the kratom trade, the practical consequence is the loss of legal market access across Kansas. Manufacturers, distributors, and retailers, including smoke shops, vape stores, and gas-station vendors, will not lawfully be able to sell products containing mitragynine or 7-OH after July 1, the Lawrence Journal-World reported, and those that continue face criminal exposure. The reporting also noted that possession prohibitions push interstate sales, such as Kansas residents ordering from out-of-state vendors, into a legally uncertain area.

Kansas's full Schedule I approach stands apart from the path other states are taking on the same date. [Gov. Laura Kelly signed the state's 7-OH scheduling bill in May][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax], and the July 1 prohibition now covers kratom broadly. Virginia, by contrast, is [adopting product standards and an under-21 sales limit][virginia-kratom-restrictions-take-effect-july-one] rather than a ban that takes effect the same day.
