Virginia's new kratom restrictions take effect July 1, 2026, prohibiting sales to anyone under 21 and imposing a set of product standards rather than an outright ban, [WTKR reported](https://www.wtkr.com/news/politics/new-virginia-laws-taking-effect-july-1-styrofoam-bans-assault-weapons-marijuana-convictions). The measure is one of several state actions on kratom that go live the same day, alongside the full bans [taking effect in Tennessee and Kansas][tennessee-kansas-bans-take-effect-july-one].

## What the restrictions cover

According to WTKR, the law prohibits the sale of kratom products to anyone under 21 and bars products that meet any of a list of conditions. Under the reported terms, a kratom product may not lack an ingredient label, be stored in areas accessible to consumers, contain any synthesized material or 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), be capable of being injected or vaporized, be packaged in ways that appeal to children, or contain solvent levels above pharmacopeial limits.

The framework treats kratom as a consumer product subject to labeling, packaging, and product-form rules. WTKR described Virginia's legislature as adopting regulatory restrictions rather than a full ban, placing the state among those that have chosen to channel kratom into a regulated market with conditions on how it can be sold.

## The 7-OH and synthetic-product line

The Virginia rules single out 7-hydroxymitragynine and synthesized material for exclusion, according to WTKR. That distinction tracks a pattern in recent state action. 7-OH occurs naturally as a trace alkaloid in kratom leaf, but it is present at far higher concentrations in extracts and in synthetic or semi-synthetic preparations, which have been the focus of regulators this year. Florida's [expanded emergency rule signed June 22][florida-emergency-rule-7oh-schedule-one] drew a similar line, targeting concentrated and synthetic 7-OH formulas while leaving natural kratom leaf unscheduled.

For manufacturers and retailers, the practical effect in Virginia is that products enhanced with 7-OH, synthetic blends, or novel delivery formats such as vapes and injectables would fall outside the legal market, while labeled leaf-based products sold to adults 21 and older could remain. WTKR's report did not detail the enforcement mechanism or penalties attached to the new rules.

## A split with the same-day bans

Virginia's product-standard approach contrasts with the prohibition model going into effect the same day in two other states. Tennessee and Kansas both move to full bans on July 1, with Kansas placing kratom and 7-OH in Schedule I and [local officials there preparing for enforcement][kansas-localities-prepare-july-one-kratom-ban]. The result is a single date on which three states adopt sharply different postures: two banning kratom across the board, and Virginia keeping it on the market under age limits, labeling rules, and a bar on synthetic and 7-OH-containing formulations.
