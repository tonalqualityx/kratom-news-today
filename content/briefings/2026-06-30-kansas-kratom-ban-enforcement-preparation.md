Kansas law enforcement and public health officials are laying groundwork for the state's kratom ban, which takes effect tomorrow under House Bill 2365. The law places kratom and 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), a kratom alkaloid that is a current focus of federal regulatory attention, in Schedule I of the state controlled substances act, the same classification Kansas applies to heroin, psilocybin, and marijuana.

The Douglas County District Attorney's Office told the [Lawrence Journal-World](https://www2.ljworld.com/news/public-safety/2026/jun/27/kansas-kratom-ban-begins-july-1-and-law-enforcement-and-health-officials-in-lawrence-are-preparing/) it will treat kratom and 7-OH possession cases "like other Schedule I drugs with no carve-outs," reviewing cases on a case-by-case basis. The statement signals that prosecutors do not plan to treat kratom differently from other controlled substances in the state's Schedule I, a position that will affect both individual possession cases and retail enforcement.

State data cited in the Journal-World's reporting show kratom-related hospital visits in Kansas rising from approximately 50 in 2024 to 164 in 2025. Local officials pointed to those numbers as part of their justification for the [ban signed by Governor Kelly earlier this year][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban].

Health officials quoted by the Journal-World said they are preparing for treatment needs that may follow the ban's implementation. Those officials told the paper that people dependent on kratom could face "painful withdrawals if they suddenly stop taking it," and that treatment providers are planning for that scenario. Smoke shops in Lawrence and other Kansas cities that currently sell kratom products are preparing to pull inventory ahead of the July 1 deadline.

The Kansas ban arrives alongside Tennessee's full kratom prohibition, also effective July 1, which KNT [covered last week][tennessee-kansas-bans-take-effect-july-one]. The two bans taking effect on the same day mark an unusual concentration of state-level kratom prohibitions.

## Washington state cities continue municipal ban pattern

In Washington state, the Ephrata City Council scheduled a first reading for July 1 on a proposed ordinance that would ban the sale, distribution, and advertising of kratom and 7-OH products within city limits, the [Columbia Basin Herald reported](https://columbiabasinherald.com/news/2026/jun/29/ephrata-city-council-considers-ordinance-banning-kratom/) on June 29. If adopted, the ordinance would take effect 60 days after adoption and publication.

Ephrata would join a growing cluster of Grant County municipalities that have already enacted kratom bans. Warden, Royal City, and Mattawa have adopted bans, and Othello in neighboring Adams County has enacted a similar ordinance. The Grant County Board of Health passed a resolution in May 2026 urging cities and the county to restrict or ban kratom sales until a comprehensive regulatory framework is in place, citing concerns about concentrated kratom products in retail settings, a rise in exposure calls, and incidents involving children.

The council packet, according to the Herald, references a proposed Washington state bill from the 2026 legislative session that would have established testing and labeling requirements for kratom products. That bill did not pass, leaving municipalities to act independently in the absence of a statewide framework. The packet also notes that the FDA has not approved kratom for medical use and has issued warnings about potential risks associated with kratom products.

## Florida AG announces 7-OH enforcement push

In a separate development, Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier announced a crackdown on products featuring 7-OH at a [news conference in Tampa on June 29](https://www.aol.com/articles/florida-attorney-general-uthmeier-announces-145431000.html), according to AOL News. The announcement adds Florida to the list of states where attorneys general have taken public enforcement positions on concentrated 7-OH products in recent months.

Florida's existing Kratom Consumer Protection Act (Florida Statutes Section 500.92) already prohibits sale of kratom products to anyone under 21 and directs the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services to adopt rules administering the statute. Uthmeier's announcement appears to target concentrated 7-OH products specifically, though details of the enforcement scope were not fully described in the available reporting.

The enforcement actions across Kansas, Washington, and Florida reflect different regulatory approaches to the same set of concerns. Kansas chose full Schedule I prohibition of all kratom products. Ephrata and its neighbors are pursuing municipal sales bans. Florida is using its existing consumer protection framework to target specific product categories. An [updated tracker from Ohio State's Moritz College of Law](https://moritzlaw.osu.edu/faculty-and-research/drug-enforcement-and-policy-center/research-and-grants/policy-and-data-analyses/regulating-kratom), published June 29, noted that 17 states have now adopted some version of the American Kratom Association's model Kratom Consumer Protection Act, while a separate group of states has moved toward outright bans or Schedule I classification.
