Halfway through 2026, kratom regulation in the United States has reached its most active period since the DEA's withdrawn 2016 scheduling attempt. Seven states ban the plant outright, and Tennessee becomes the eighth on July 1. Thirty states and the District of Columbia regulate kratom in some form, according to the [Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association's summary of state laws](https://legislativeanalysis.org/kratom-summary-of-state-laws/). The FDA has [recommended that the DEA schedule concentrated 7-hydroxymitragynine products](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers) while explicitly exempting natural leaf, and the DEA's review remains open. And Rhode Island became the first state to reverse a ban.

This report maps the regulatory landscape as it stands on June 12, 2026: the federal posture, the dividing line between leaf and 7-OH, the ban states, the regulated middle, the courts, the enforcement wave, and the data driving all of it.

## Key Numbers at Midyear

- **7 states** ban kratom outright today; **Tennessee makes 8 on July 1**.
- **30 states and the District of Columbia** regulate kratom in some form, per the Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association (January 2026).
- **1 state**, Rhode Island, has reversed a ban, the first in the nation to move from prohibition back to a regulated market.
- **A roughly 1,200 percent increase** in kratom-related poison center calls from 2015 to 2025, per CDC surveillance.
- **22 entities** are under civil investigative demand in Missouri's attorney general probe, the broadest state enforcement action to date.
- **95 percent retail compliance** and more than **$5 million in products seized** under California's enforcement program, without a kratom statute on the books.
- **Two federal deadlines inside three weeks:** Florida's emergency 7-OH order expires June 30, and Tennessee's ban, Georgia's restrictions, and Idaho Falls' local ban all take effect July 1.

## The Federal Picture: A Recommendation, Not a Rule

Kratom remains unscheduled under the federal Controlled Substances Act. The only direct federal scheduling attempt, the DEA's 2016 move to place mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine in Schedule I, was [withdrawn after an unusually large volume of public comments](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2016/08/31/2016-20803/schedules-of-controlled-substances-temporary-placement-of-mitragynine-and-7-hydroxymitragynine-into). That status quo held for nearly a decade. It is now under more pressure than at any point since.

The FDA [recommended on June 1 that the DEA schedule concentrated 7-OH products][fda-recommends-scheduling-7oh-kansas-signs-ban] under the Controlled Substances Act, pairing the recommendation with warning letters to seven companies distributing 7-OH tablets, gummies, drink mixes, and shots. The agency framed the action as "specifically targeting 7-OH" and stated it is "not focused on natural kratom leaf products," [according to the FDA's announcement](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-takes-steps-restrict-7-oh-opioid-products-threatening-american-consumers). The DEA has final scheduling authority, and placement under the Controlled Substances Act's scheduling procedures requires rulemaking with public comment. No final rule or temporary scheduling order had been published as of this report.

Congress has not introduced kratom-specific legislation in the 119th Congress, based on Congress.gov records. Congressional pressure has instead come through correspondence: a bipartisan letter led by Senator Pete Ricketts [called on the FDA to address what it described as a kratom scheduling loophole][senators-push-full-kratom-scheduling-states-crack-down], urging attention to the plant beyond 7-OH alone, [according to the senator's office](https://www.ricketts.senate.gov/news/press-releases/ricketts-letter-calls-on-fda-to-address-kratom-scheduling-loophole/). The White House's [2026 National Drug Control Strategy cited kratom-linked deaths][white-house-drug-strategy-kratom-massachusetts-local-bans] among emerging drug threats.

The executive branch has not spoken with one voice. President Trump [floated federal approval for what he called "natural 7-OH"][trump-floats-federal-approval-natural-7-oh] in May, a pivot from his administration's scheduling push that [split the industry][industry-splits-over-trump-natural-7-oh-remarks]. The American Kratom Association called vendor framing of the remarks "a big con job," according to Pain News Network. The Daily Beast separately reported that DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin [holds up to $1 million in Botanic Tonics stock][dhs-secretary-mullin-holds-stake-in-kratom-company], a disclosure that landed while federal 7-OH policy remains unsettled.

Two other federal developments cut in different directions. The NIH [cleared the way for the first human trial of mitragynine][nih-clears-mitragynine-opioid-use-disorder-trial] as an experimental treatment for opioid use disorder, [per the agency's June 1 release](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/nih-research-clears-way-study-experimental-treatment-opioid-use-disorder). And the American Medical Association's House of Delegates took up Resolution 201, "Prohibit and Regulate 7-Hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) Kratom Products," at its annual meeting that concluded June 10, with the resolution submitted by the Oklahoma, Mississippi, and South Carolina delegations, [according to meeting documents](https://www.ama-assn.org/system/files/a26-refcomm-b-report.pdf). The American Kratom Association [responded by urging the AMA](https://www.accessnewswire.com/newsroom/en/healthcare-and-pharmaceutical/american-kratom-association-calls-on-ama-to-properly-identify-the-rea-1175916) to distinguish what it called chemically manipulated 7-OH opioid products from natural leaf kratom.

## The Line That Defines 2026: Leaf Versus 7-OH

Nearly every significant regulatory action this year draws the same distinction the FDA drew: natural kratom leaf on one side, concentrated or synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine on the other. 7-OH occurs in trace amounts in raw leaf. The products driving regulatory attention contain the alkaloid at concentrations far above what occurs naturally, in many cases through chemical conversion of mitragynine.

States have operationalized the distinction in different ways. Ohio's [permanent rule banning synthetic kratom products][ohio-synthetic-kratom-ban-takes-effect] took effect in May after clearing the legislature's rule-review committee, replacing an emergency rule, while the state's Board of Pharmacy has weighed whether restrictions should extend to natural leaf, according to the Statehouse News Bureau. Kansas Governor Laura Kelly [signed House Bill 2365][kansas-signs-7-oh-ban-washington-weighs-kratom-tax], a 7-OH scheduling measure, [per the governor's office](https://www.governor.ks.gov/Home/Components/News/News/1016/55). The New Hampshire House [passed Senate Bill 557][new-hampshire-kratom-bill-missouri-ag-expands-probe], targeting enhanced 7-OH products. New York's legislature [unanimously passed the Synthetic Kratom Kills Act][new-york-legislature-passes-synthetic-kratom-7oh-ban] in June, a bill that would prohibit lab-produced 7-OH products while preserving the natural leaf market; it awaits Governor Kathy Hochul's signature.

The cap-based version of this approach predates the 2026 wave. Colorado and Mississippi both wrote explicit 7-OH ceilings into Kratom Consumer Protection Act statutes in 2025. Colorado's Senate Bill 25-072, the Daniel Bregger Act, caps 7-OH at 2 percent of total alkaloid content, bans the manufacture and distribution of synthetic and semi-synthetic kratom, and restricts sales to adults 21 and older, [according to the Colorado General Assembly](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb25-072). Mississippi's House Bill 1077, [signed by Governor Tate Reeves in April 2025 and effective that July](https://billstatus.ls.state.ms.us/documents/2025/html/HB/1000-1099/HB1077SG.htm), set a tighter ceiling, prohibiting kratom products that contain more than 1 percent 7-OH, and added behind-the-counter placement, a 21-and-older age limit, and an excise tax.

The industry's main advocacy organizations have largely endorsed the split. The American Kratom Association [issued a policy advisory urging state attorneys general to enforce existing law against 7-OH products][aka-urges-ag-enforcement-7oh-florida-ban-expiry], and the Global Kratom Coalition has published [its own statement distinguishing 7-OH products from leaf kratom](https://globalkratomcoalition.org/statement-on-7-hydroxymitragynine-products). Manufacturers have responded to the patchwork commercially as well: the Philadelphia Inquirer [reported that kratom companies are reformulating 7-OH products][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate] to sidestep pending state bans.

## The Ban States: Seven Today, Eight in July

Connecticut became the most recent full-ban state when regulations designating kratom and its derivatives, including 7-OH, as Schedule I controlled substances received final approval on April 2, [according to the state's Department of Consumer Protection](https://portal.ct.gov/dcp/knowledge-base/articles/drug-control/patients/kratom-update-october-2025), which described Connecticut as the seventh state to do so. Retailers [pulled product from shelves across the state][georgia-kratom-law-connecticut-schedule-one] in the weeks that followed.

The other six are Alabama, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Vermont, and Wisconsin, per the [LAPPA summary](https://legislativeanalysis.org/kratom-summary-of-state-laws/). Most date to scheduling decisions made in the 2010s; Louisiana joined in August 2025.

Tennessee makes eight on July 1, when the [full prohibition signed by Governor Bill Lee in May][tennessee-kratom-ban-missouri-alabama-ag-enforcement] takes effect. Michigan could follow: the state House [passed an outright ban][new-york-signs-kratom-age-limit-michigan-house-passes-ban] in May, and the bill is pending in the Senate.

Rhode Island moved the opposite way. A ban state since 2017, it replaced its prohibition with a regulatory framework effective April 1 under the [Rhode Island Kratom Act](https://webserver.rilegislature.gov/Statutes/TITLE21/21-28.12/21-28.12-6.htm), which establishes licensing for manufacturers, importers, distributors, and retailers. The state's Department of Health [approved its first kratom manufacturer license on June 5][rhode-island-first-kratom-license-american-shaman-missouri-suspension], per WJAR. The Rockefeller Institute of Government, in [its survey of the policy landscape](https://rockinst.org/blog/the-evolving-kratom-policy-landscape/), identified Rhode Island as the first state to move from full prohibition back to regulated-legal status.

## The Regulated Middle: Consumer Protection Acts and Their Successors

Between the ban states and the unregulated states sits the largest group: jurisdictions with Kratom Consumer Protection Act frameworks or equivalents, generally built on age limits, labeling, alkaloid disclosure, adulteration bans, and in newer versions registration and testing. Utah enacted the first KCPA in 2019, and states including Arizona, Georgia, Nevada, Florida, Texas, and West Virginia followed with statutes of their own, per the LAPPA summary.

The 2026 trend within this group is toward stricter second-generation frameworks:

- **Utah** replaced its original KCPA with the stricter Kratom Regulation Act, which [took effect May 6 as manufacturers reformulated products to comply][utah-kratom-act-takes-effect-manufacturers-reformulate]. The law bans high-concentration extracts and kratom products mixed with other substances and is now the subject of federal litigation (see below).
- **Georgia's** new kratom restrictions [carry a July 1 effective date][georgia-kratom-law-connecticut-schedule-one].
- **North Carolina** set a [July 2026 licensing deadline][california-kratom-sales-bill-north-carolina-licensing] for kratom businesses, according to the UNC School of Government's legislative reporting service.
- **New York** enacted an under-21 sales restriction and warning-label requirements [signed by Governor Hochul on June 3](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-signs-two-pieces-landmark-legislation-protect-new-yorkers-against-harmful), with the broader synthetic-kratom ban awaiting her signature.

The pending-bill docket is long. California's [AB 1088](https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_202520260ab1088), which would add kratom and 7-OH products to the Sherman Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Law with alkaloid caps, an under-21 restriction, registration, and a retail tax, was re-referred to the Assembly Health and Revenue and Taxation committees on June 10 and remains in progress. [Delaware is weighing controlled-substance classification][arizona-kratom-bill-delaware-weighs-ban] through SB 262, while Arizona's legislature advanced a regulation bill. Ohio House members [introduced a Kratom Consumer Protection Act][missouri-ag-expands-kratom-probe-ohio-consumer-protection-act] even as the state's synthetic ban took hold, and [South Dakota and Minnesota advanced restrictions][litigation-landscape-south-dakota-minnesota-restrictions] late in their sessions. Washington considered a KCPA with state-preemption provisions ([HB 2291](https://app.leg.wa.gov/BillSummary/?BillNumber=2291&Year=2026)), and Wyoming's [SF0056](https://www.wyoleg.gov/Legislation/2026/SF0056) would restrict sales to those under 21.

The nearest deadline is Florida's. The state's emergency order scheduling 7-OH at concentrations of one percent or higher, [issued by the attorney general in August 2025](https://www.clickorlando.com/news/florida/2025/08/13/this-drug-is-now-banned-from-florida-in-new-emergency-order/), [expires June 30][aka-urges-ag-enforcement-7oh-florida-ban-expiry]. The legislature adjourned in March without codifying it, and the Rockefeller Institute noted the scheduling may simply lapse. If it does, Florida's 7-OH scheduling ends without any legislature or agency having voted to end it.

## The Courts: Preemption, Enforcement, and Product Liability

The case with the broadest implications is the Global Kratom Coalition and Botanic Tonics' federal challenge to Utah's Kratom Regulation Act. Judge Howard C. Nielson Jr. of the U.S. District Court for the District of Utah [denied the plaintiffs' request to block enforcement on May 4](https://www.courthousenews.com/federal-judge-rejects-bid-to-halt-enforcement-of-utahs-kratom-regulation-act/), rejecting the argument that the federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act preempts the state law. Plaintiffs [filed an appeal with the Tenth Circuit](https://www.deseret.com/lifestyle/2026/05/11/challenge-to-kratom-law-shut-down-appeal/), per Deseret News, and the law remains in effect during litigation. A ruling either way will shape how far states can go: every second-generation state framework rests on the same authority Utah is defending.

No state office has announced more kratom enforcement actions this year than Missouri's. Attorney General Catherine Hanaway [launched an investigation of major kratom manufacturers and retailers](https://ago.mo.gov/attorney-general-hanaway-launches-investigation-into-major-kratom-manufacturers-and-retailers-for-potentially-dangerous-and-illegal-practices/) with six civil investigative demands, then [expanded it with demands to 22 supply-chain entities][missouri-ag-expands-kratom-probe-ohio-consumer-protection-act], per her office. A Jackson County judge denied the state's bid to halt 7-OH sales by CBD American Shaman in May, according to KCUR, but the company [agreed in June to suspend kratom sales in Missouri][rhode-island-first-kratom-license-american-shaman-missouri-suspension] while the dispute proceeds, per KCTV5. The office separately sued manufacturer EDP Kratom in May.

Elsewhere on the enforcement map: Alabama Attorney General Steve Marshall [issued statewide cease-and-desist orders](https://www.alabamaag.gov/alabama-attorney-general-issues-statewide-cease-and-desist-order-targeting-illegal-kratom-products/) on March 25 targeting retailers selling kratom products, which are already Schedule I under Alabama law. Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton [sued kratom retailers](https://www.texasattorneygeneral.gov/news/releases/attorney-general-paxton-sues-kratom-retailers-selling-products-containing-nearly-fifty-times-legal) whose products allegedly contained nearly 50 times the 7-OH limit in the state's consumer protection act. Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes published a [consumer alert on kratom](https://www.azag.gov/ag-mayes-consumer-alert-kratom). And California built the most sweeping enforcement program of any state without a kratom statute: after the Department of Public Health declared kratom and 7-OH products illegal to sell or manufacture under the Sherman Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act in October 2025, the state reported [95 percent retail compliance, with more than $5 million in products seized](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2026/03/03/governor-newsom-announces-95-compliance-with-prohibition-of-illegal-kratom-products/), per Governor Gavin Newsom's office. The state has since [sued a manufacturer for allegedly defying product embargoes][california-sues-kratom-manufacturer-litigation-risk-grows], according to the Sacramento Bee, and U.S. Marshals [seized kratom in a federal action in the Central District of California](https://www.justice.gov/usao-cdca/pr/kratom-seized-california-united-states-marshals-service) in May.

On the product-liability side, the federal government [dropped its case against Botanic Tonics][botanic-tonics-case-dismissed-new-york-alabama-kratom], maker of the kratom tonic Feel Free, in early June, as covered previously by KNT. Private litigation is moving the other way. A Palm Beach County family [filed a wrongful-death suit over Feel Free][idaho-falls-kratom-ban-botanic-tonics-wrongful-death] in June, per CBS 12, one of a growing set of cases. Mayer Brown's [analysis of the emerging kratom litigation landscape](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/05/the-emerging-kratom-litigation-landscape-and-implications-for-similarly-situated-manufacturers) mapped the exposure for manufacturers across wrongful-death, consumer-fraud, and labeling theories, and [KNT's coverage of that analysis][litigation-landscape-south-dakota-minnesota-restrictions] noted the firm's view that state-law variation itself is a litigation driver.

## The Local Layer: Cities Move Where States Have Not

In states without kratom statutes, the regulatory action has moved to city councils and county boards. Washington State is the clearest example: with no statewide law, [Spokane banned kratom sales in March, and other local governments have followed][washington-local-governments-fill-kratom-regulatory-vacuum], a pattern documented by the Municipal Research and Services Center. Idaho Falls [voted unanimously in June to ban kratom sales](https://idahonews.com/news/local/idaho-falls-bans-kratom-sales-starting-july-1-after-coroner-links-it-to-6-deaths) effective July 1, after the county coroner linked kratom to six deaths, per Idaho News. Philadelphia's City Council [introduced bills targeting smoke shops that sell kratom and similar products][philadelphia-council-clashes-with-kratom-advocates-over-smoke-shop-bills], drawing advocacy pushback. [Massachusetts towns have moved on local restrictions][white-house-drug-strategy-kratom-massachusetts-local-bans] as well.

The local layer matters beyond its direct reach. Statewide frameworks under consideration in Washington and elsewhere include preemption provisions because the patchwork now exists within states as well as among them, according to the Association of Washington Cities.

## The Data Behind the Push

Three datasets recur in nearly every legislative hearing and enforcement announcement this year.

The CDC's Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report on [kratom-related exposures reported to U.S. poison centers from 2015 to 2025][cdc-kratom-poison-center-calls-surge] documented a roughly 1,200 percent increase in kratom-related calls over the decade. A separate peer-reviewed study summarized by the [Medical College of Wisconsin](https://www.mcw.edu/newsroom/news-articles/kratom-use-is-surging-in-the-us-with-life-changing-consequences) reported exposures rose from 19 in 2010 to 1,242 in 2023, with severe outcomes rising from zero to 158 over the same period; the authors also reported that states with bans had lower exposure rates than states with consumer-protection rules or no regulation.

On mortality, California's March announcement [cited a CDC analysis](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2026/03/03/governor-newsom-announces-95-compliance-with-prohibition-of-illegal-kratom-products/) finding kratom implicated, at least in part, in 846 fatal overdose cases across 30 states and the District of Columbia in 2022, and a state analysis implicating kratom in 242 California deaths from 2020 through 2022. Both figures count deaths in which kratom was implicated alongside other substances; the California analysis attributed 27 of the 242 deaths solely to kratom.

The research picture is mixed. The same month the AMA took up a resolution to restrict 7-OH products, the NIH [cleared a first-in-human trial of mitragynine for opioid use disorder][nih-clears-mitragynine-opioid-use-disorder-trial]. Regulators are restricting one kratom alkaloid while federal researchers study another as a potential treatment.

## What to Watch in the Second Half of 2026

- **The DEA's response to the FDA's 7-OH scheduling recommendation.** Any proposed rule triggers public comment and would be the first federal scheduling action on a kratom alkaloid since 2016.
- **June 30: Florida's emergency 7-OH order expires.** Absent new action, the scheduling lapses.
- **July 1: Tennessee's full ban and Georgia's new restrictions take effect**, and Idaho Falls' local ban begins.
- **Governor Hochul's decision on New York's Synthetic Kratom Kills Act**, passed unanimously by both chambers.
- **Michigan's Senate**, which holds the House-passed ban that would make Michigan the ninth prohibition state.
- **The Tenth Circuit's handling of the Utah preemption appeal**, the case most likely to define the outer limits of state authority.
- **California's AB 1088 and North Carolina's July licensing deadline**, the two largest pending state frameworks.

The pattern across all of it is consistent. Federal policy remains unsettled, and the states are answering the same question in different ways: where is the line between natural leaf kratom and the concentrated 7-OH derivatives that regulators increasingly treat as a novel opioid? In 2026, nearly every legislature, agency, and courtroom touching kratom is answering some version of that question. The answers, so far, do not match.

## Methodology

**Scope and window.** This report covers federal, state, and notable local kratom regulatory activity in the United States through June 12, 2026. It is the first edition in a continuing series; a year-end 2026 edition is planned.

**Sources.** Claims are sourced to primary documents wherever they exist: statutes and bill texts, agency announcements (FDA, DEA, NIH, CDC, state health departments), attorney general releases, court records, and the Federal Register. Aggregate state counts (the "30 states and DC" baseline and the roster of pre-2026 ban states) come from the Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association's January 2026 summary of state laws, supplemented by the Rockefeller Institute of Government's policy survey. Where reporting from other outlets is the source, the outlet is named in the text. The full source list appears at the end of this page.

**The dataset.** The downloadable table ([CSV](/assets/data/state-of-kratom-regulation-2026.csv)) lists each state with a regulatory status or 2026 action documented in this report's sourcing: 32 states as of this edition. Each row carries a status category, a dated summary, and a primary source URL. States absent from the table had no documented 2026 activity in our sourcing; their baseline status is covered by the LAPPA summary linked above. Status categories (full ban, regulated framework, 7-OH-specific restriction, enforcement activity, pending legislation, local action) describe the dominant documented posture and are assigned by the editor.

**Production and corrections.** Kratom News Today reports are produced through the AI-assisted editorial workflow described in our [editorial policy](/editorial-policy/), with review and final responsibility by the Managing Editor. Errors are corrected per our [corrections policy](/corrections/), and material corrections to this report will be noted on this page.

**Updates.** This page's URL is permanent. The report text reflects June 12, 2026; if states act between editions, updates appear in our daily [regulation coverage](/regulation/) and are folded into the next edition.
