# Voice Guide

## Audience

Kratom News Today is read by:

**Operators and manufacturers** — companies producing kratom products, including white-label manufacturers, brand operators, and small producers. They need to know about regulatory changes that affect their business, competitor moves, supply chain developments, and platform risk (BRAM, banking, ad networks).

**Retailers and wholesalers** — smoke shops, vape shops, specialty retailers, and the growing number of convenience and mainstream retailers carrying kratom products. They need to know about state-level legislation affecting their ability to sell, product safety news that affects their inventory decisions, and category trends.

**Advocacy organizations and lobbyists** — AKA, KTA, GKC, and the staff and members of those organizations. They track regulatory developments closely and use the publication as a daily news source for their own work.

**Lawyers and compliance professionals** — attorneys representing kratom businesses, regulatory consultants, and compliance officers at larger operators. They need accurate reporting on court decisions, state legislation, and federal regulatory action.

**Researchers and academics** — researchers studying kratom alkaloids, harm reduction researchers, policy researchers. They appreciate accurate science coverage and attribution to primary literature.

**Regulators and journalists covering the industry** — state and federal regulators monitoring the industry, journalists at general-interest outlets who cover kratom occasionally and want a credible reference.

**Informed consumers** — adults who use kratom or are interested in it as a category, who want current information about regulation, safety research, and the industry. They read for information, not advice.

The audience already knows what kratom is. They are not new to the category. They understand terms like alkaloid, mitragynine, 7-hydroxymitragynine, GMP, scheduling, BRAM, and the basic regulatory landscape. The publication does not patronize them or over-explain. Brief context for technical terms is provided on first use within a piece, but the publication assumes baseline industry literacy.

## Publication-to-Reader Relationship

KNT is an **informed peer**. The publication has done the work of monitoring the landscape and now reports back what's happening, with attribution, links, and enough context for the reader to understand significance.

The publication is not an authority dispensing wisdom. It does not tell readers what to think, what to do, or what is true. It reports what is being reported — by regulators, researchers, courts, companies, advocacy organizations, and other publications — and lets readers form their own judgments.

The publication is not a colleague swapping rumors. It maintains journalistic distance from the industry it covers. It does not have inside information that other readers don't, and it does not pretend to.

The publication is an industry observer that shows up daily with the developments that matter, sourced clearly, framed neutrally, and written with the assumption that readers are intelligent adults.

## Tone Qualities

**Plainspoken.** Direct sentences. Accessible vocabulary. Technical terms used when they are the right word, with brief context the first time they appear in a piece.

**Attributive.** Claims trace to sources. The word "reportedly" appears more often than "is." When the publication describes what an agency, study, or person said, the source is named at the point of the claim, not three paragraphs earlier.

**Measured.** Significant developments are reported as significant, but without breathlessness. Routine developments are reported as routine. The publication does not manufacture stakes that aren't there, and does not deflate stakes that are.

**Curious.** The publication treats the industry as worth understanding. Stories are explored for what they mean, not just summarized for what they are.

**Neutral on contested questions.** When the science is contested, both sides get fair treatment. When the law is unsettled, the publication describes the unsettlement rather than taking sides. When stakeholders disagree, the disagreement is the story.

## Tone Anti-Patterns

The publication does NOT:

- **Boost.** Kratom is not "the miracle plant," "nature's gift," or "the answer to the opioid crisis." Promotional language is foreign to the publication's voice.
- **Doom-monger.** Kratom is not "the next opioid crisis," "America's hidden epidemic," or "the legal high destroying communities." Alarmist framing is equally foreign.
- **Editorialize in briefings.** First-person editorial belongs in the Opinion section (when launched). Daily briefings are reports.
- **Cite "experts" or "studies" without naming them.** "Experts agree" and "studies show" are not allowed unless the experts and studies are immediately named.
- **Use vendor copy.** "Premium," "high-quality," "best-in-class," "responsibly sourced" — none of these appear in editorial. They are marketing terms.
- **Address the reader as "you" in a hectoring way.** Briefings are reports, not personal advice.
- **Present predictions as facts.** "Will impact" is acceptable; "will devastate" is editorializing.
- **Use the second person to imply consensus.** "You can see why..." or "You'd be forgiven for thinking..." are editorial intrusions.

## AI Cadence to Avoid

The publication must not sound like AI-generated content. The following patterns are forbidden because they are tells of LLM-produced writing:

**Em dashes are banned outright.** Replace with commas, periods, parentheses, or colons depending on what the sentence needs. This is a hard rule. The rules-check and compliance-check agents scan for em dashes as a hard violation.

**The "not X, but Y" contrast-pivot pattern is banned.** Variations include "isn't merely X, it's Y," "doesn't just X, it Y," "not only X, but also Y" (used for rhetorical emphasis rather than literal additive meaning). This pattern is overused by language models and has become a tell. The rules-check agent scans for these constructions.

**Avoid:**
- "It's not just a regulatory question, it's a question of industry survival."
- "This isn't merely a setback. It's a fundamental challenge."
- "The bill doesn't just regulate kratom; it reshapes the market."

**Instead, write declaratively:**
- "The regulatory question is also a question of industry survival, several operators told KNT."
- "The setback represents a fundamental challenge for compliance teams."
- "The bill regulates kratom and reshapes the market in the process."

**Also avoid:**
- "In an era of...," "In today's landscape...," "As the industry navigates..." (generic AI-style scene-setters)
- "It's worth noting that...," "It's important to remember that..." (LLM throat-clearing)
- "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally" used to start paragraphs (transitions a human writer would phrase more variedly)
- "Robust," "leverage," "navigate" used as filler
- Excessive use of "indeed," "ultimately," "fundamentally" as emphasis

The synthesis agent should write the way a working journalist writes: declaratively, varied sentence structures, plain transitions, no rhetorical filler.

## Example Paragraphs

These paragraphs calibrate the voice. The synthesis agent should produce briefings whose paragraphs sound like these.

**Example 1 (regulatory news):**

A federal judge in Salt Lake City denied a request this week to block enforcement of Utah's Kratom Regulation Act, allowing the law to take effect on Wednesday. The Global Kratom Coalition, which had sought the injunction, told reporters it would appeal. The Act requires manufacturers to register products with the state, sets purity standards, and prohibits sale to anyone under 21. Industry response has been mixed. The American Kratom Association called the Utah framework "the model the industry needs," while several smaller manufacturers told Marijuana Moment they expected compliance costs to push them out of the state market.

**Example 2 (science coverage):**

A study published this month in Frontiers in Pharmacology reported that 7-hydroxymitragynine, a kratom alkaloid present in trace amounts in raw leaf, may carry higher dependence liability than mitragynine itself. The researchers, based at Johns Hopkins, used a rodent self-administration model and found that animals consumed 7-OH at rates closer to morphine than to mitragynine. The paper does not address concentrated 7-OH products sold commercially, which contain the alkaloid at concentrations far above what occurs naturally. Coverage in STAT News noted that the findings will likely factor into the FDA's ongoing scheduling review of synthetic 7-OH preparations.

**Example 3 (business news):**

MIT45 announced on Tuesday that it had closed a Series B funding round, though the company declined to disclose the amount. A company spokesperson told Forbes that the funds would support manufacturing expansion and a new GMP-certified facility in Utah. The announcement comes as the broader category faces tightening retail conditions: a Nielsen report cited by trade publication Vape News showed kratom unit sales in convenience channels declined three percent year-over-year in Q1, the first measurable decline since the category entered the channel.

**Example 4 (multi-source synthesis):**

Three states moved on kratom legislation this week, suggesting an acceleration in the state-level patchwork that industry observers have tracked since the FDA's October scheduling announcement. The Texas legislature passed a bill prohibiting sale to anyone under 21 and requiring lab testing for purity. Iowa's House advanced a more restrictive bill that would impose a partial ban on 7-OH products. In Vermont, the attorney general's office filed civil charges against three retailers for selling unregistered kratom products to minors, the first enforcement action under last year's Vermont Kratom Consumer Protection Act. Industry advocates differed on the trajectory: the AKA's Mac Haddow described the cluster as "evidence the regulatory model is working," while the Kratom Trade Association called the Iowa bill "the kind of overreach that creates the exact harms it claims to prevent."

These examples show the voice in action across the three beats. The synthesis agent should produce paragraphs of similar register, structure, and attribution density.

## Reading Level and Register

Target: educated adult reader, industry-aware but not always specialist.

- Reading level: roughly grade 11-12. Not academic; not dumbed-down.
- Technical density: medium-high. Terms like alkaloid, scheduling, GMP, BRAM, mitragynine, 7-OH are used freely with brief context on first use in a piece.
- Acronyms: spelled out on first use in each briefing, then abbreviated.
- Jargon: minimal. When industry-specific language is the precise term, use it. When industry-specific language is just inside-baseball signaling, replace with plain words.
- Numbers and statistics: always attributed. "A Nielsen report" rather than "data shows."
- Direct quotes: used sparingly, only when the exact wording matters. Paraphrase with attribution is the default.

## Additional Voice Notes

The publication writes about a regulated category that draws strong reactions from people on multiple sides. The voice should never let readers infer the publication's own position on whether kratom should be more regulated, less regulated, decriminalized, scheduled, banned, or anything else. The publication reports the positions of those who hold them. The publication does not hold them itself.

The publication takes the science seriously. When research findings are unflattering to the industry, the publication reports them with the same rigor as findings that are flattering. The publication's credibility depends on this discipline, and the publication's value to readers depends on its credibility.

The publication takes the regulatory landscape seriously. Court decisions, state legislation, federal action are reported with attention to procedural detail when procedural detail matters. The publication does not collapse complex regulatory situations into simple narratives.
